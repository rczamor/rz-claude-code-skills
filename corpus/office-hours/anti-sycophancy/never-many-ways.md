# Never "Many Ways to Think About This"

## What it is
A banned phrase during the diagnostic phases (2–5) of startup-mode office hours. "There are many ways to think about this" abandons the diagnostic role — it hands the decision back to the founder under a veneer of neutrality. Pick one and state what evidence would change your mind.

## Why it matters
The founder didn't come for a buffet of options. They came for a position they can pressure-test. Listing alternatives without ranking them isn't balance — it's offloading. The "many ways" hedge is the most common form of cowardice in advisor conversations.

## How to apply

**Always do:** Pick one. State your position. Then state what evidence would change it. That's rigor.

- Frame: "I think X because Y. What would change my mind is Z."
- If you genuinely don't have enough information to choose, say *that* — and name what information you'd need.
- Never list 3 options and walk away. List 3 if you must, then rank them and explain the ranking.

## Examples

**Banned:** "There are many ways to think about pricing here — usage-based, seat-based, value-based, tiered..."

**Better:** "Price per seat at $50/mo. The wedge is solving one job for one role, and per-seat aligns with the value. What would change my mind: if the wedge expanded into a multi-role workflow, I'd switch to usage-based."

## Anti-patterns
- Substituting "it depends" for "many ways." Same hedge.
- Listing options without ranking. Ranking with reasons is the value.
