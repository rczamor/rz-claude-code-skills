# Q4 — Narrowest Wedge

## What it is
The fourth forcing question. It strips away the platform vision and forces the founder to name the smallest version of the product someone would pay real money for *this week* — not after the platform is built.

**Ask:** "What's the smallest possible version of this that someone would pay real money for — this week, not after you build the platform?"

## Why it matters
Founders fall in love with architecture. The "we need to build the full platform" answer is almost always a sign that the value proposition isn't sharp enough — not that the product needs to be bigger. A wedge that ships in days reveals whether anyone wants any of this. A platform that ships in months reveals nothing until it's too late.

## How to apply

**Push until you hear:** One feature. One workflow. Maybe something as simple as a weekly email or a single automation. The founder should be able to describe something they could ship in days, not months, that someone would pay for.

**Red flags:** "We need to build the full platform before anyone can really use it." "We could strip it down but then it wouldn't be differentiated." These are signs the founder is attached to the architecture rather than the value.

**Bonus push:** "What if the user didn't have to do anything at all to get value? No login, no integration, no setup. What would that look like?"

**Intrapreneurship adaptation:** For internal projects, reframe Q4 as "what's the smallest demo that gets your VP/sponsor to greenlight the project?"

## Examples

1. **Platform answer to push:** "We're building an end-to-end revenue platform." → Red flag. "What's the one thing a user would pay for this week — before any of the rest exists?"
2. **Strong answer:** "A weekly email that flags the 5 deals at risk this week, no integration needed, $200/month. Ships in 3 days." → Concrete, time-boxed, monetized, no setup tax.
3. **Stripped-down resistance:** "We could ship just the dashboard but then we wouldn't be differentiated." → "Then your differentiation isn't the platform — it's the dashboard's insight. What makes the insight worth $200/month on its own?"

## Anti-patterns
- Letting the founder describe the wedge as "the MVP version of the platform." A wedge is a standalone thing someone pays for.
- Accepting "we'd need to keep most of the architecture for it to work" — usually means the founder is attached to the architecture, not the value.
- Skipping the bonus push. "Zero setup" is often the real wedge waiting to be found.
