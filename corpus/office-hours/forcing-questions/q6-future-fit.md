# Q6 — Future-Fit

## What it is
The sixth forcing question. It tests whether the founder has a real thesis about how their users' world changes — and whether their product becomes *more essential* in that future, not just incidentally swept along by tailwinds every competitor can claim.

**Ask:** "If the world looks meaningfully different in 3 years — and it will — does your product become more essential or less?"

## Why it matters
Every founder in the same space cites the same growth-rate stat. "AI is getting better" or "the market is growing 20% per year" are not theses — they're rising tides every competitor surfs. A real future-fit answer names a specific change in the user's world and explains why that change makes *this* product more valuable, not the category in general.

## How to apply

**Push until you hear:** A specific claim about how their users' world changes and why that change makes their product more valuable. Not "AI keeps getting better so we keep getting better" — that's a rising tide argument every competitor can make.

**Red flags:** "The market is growing 20% per year." Growth rate is not a vision. "AI will make everything better." That's not a product thesis.

**Push moves:**
1. Force the founder to describe how the *user's job changes* in 3 years.
2. Then ask why that change makes *this* product more essential, not just the category.
3. If they can't answer, the product is at risk of being commoditized by the same tide that lifts everyone.

**Intrapreneurship adaptation:** For internal projects, reframe Q6 as "does this survive a reorg — or does it die when your champion leaves?" Internal initiatives that depend on a single sponsor are future-fragile.

## Examples

1. **Growth-stat answer to push:** "The market is growing 20% YoY." → "Every competitor can cite the same stat. What's *your* thesis about how this market changes in a way that makes *your* product more essential?"
2. **Strong answer:** "In 3 years, every PM will have an AI co-pilot writing first-draft specs. The bottleneck shifts from drafting to *reviewing* and *deciding*. Our product owns the review-and-decide workflow — it gets MORE valuable as drafting gets cheaper, because the volume of drafts goes up." → Specific change in the user's job; specific reason this product compounds.
3. **AI-tide answer to push:** "AI keeps getting better, so we keep getting better." → "That's true for everyone in the space. What does your user *do differently* because AI is better — and how does that change route value to you?"

## Anti-patterns
- Accepting growth-rate citations as vision. They're tailwinds, not theses.
- "AI will make everything better" — true for every competitor, useless as positioning.
- Skipping Q6 for paying-customer founders. This is exactly when the future-fit question separates fundable from commodity.
