# Q5 — Observation & Surprise

## What it is
The fifth forcing question. It tests whether the founder has actually watched a real user — silently, without coaching — and whether anything surprised them. Surprise is the signal that the founder's mental model is bumping into reality.

**Ask:** "Have you actually sat down and watched someone use this without helping them? What did they do that surprised you?"

## Why it matters
Demos are theater. Surveys lie. Guided walkthroughs teach you nothing about real usage. The only reliable way to learn what your product actually does is to sit behind a real user, bite your tongue, and watch them struggle. If nothing has surprised the founder, they're either not watching or filtering everything through their existing assumptions.

## How to apply

**Push until you hear:** A specific surprise. Something the user did that contradicted the founder's assumptions. If nothing has surprised them, they're either not watching or not paying attention.

**Red flags:** "We sent out a survey." "We did some demo calls." "Nothing surprising, it's going as expected." Surveys lie. Demos are theater. And "as expected" means filtered through existing assumptions.

**The gold:** Users doing something the product wasn't designed for. That's often the real product trying to emerge. When a founder describes a surprising emergent use, follow that thread — it's frequently the wedge they didn't know they had.

## Examples

1. **Survey answer to push:** "We did a survey and 80% said they'd use it." → Surveys measure stated intent, not behavior. "Have you watched one person use it for an hour without helping?"
2. **Strong answer:** "We thought users would explore the dashboard first, but everyone went straight to the export button and never came back. They were using us as a one-shot data extractor, not an analytics tool." → Specific, contradicted the founder's mental model, points to the real product.
3. **"As expected" answer to push:** "Nothing surprising, the flows we designed are the flows they use." → "Either you're not watching long enough, or your design is so constrained users can't surprise you. Watch for an hour with no instructions and tell me what happens."

## Anti-patterns
- Treating demo calls as observation. A demo is the founder driving; observation is the user driving.
- Accepting "nothing surprised me" as a green light. Surprise is the deliverable.
- Discarding emergent surprising usage as misuse. It's often the real product.
