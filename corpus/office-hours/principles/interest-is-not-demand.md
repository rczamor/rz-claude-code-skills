# Interest is Not Demand

## What it is
A non-negotiable operating principle in startup-mode office hours. Waitlists, signups, "that's interesting" — none of it counts. Behavior counts. Money counts. Panic when it breaks counts. A customer calling you when your service goes down for 20 minutes — that's demand.

## Why it matters
Pre-product startups die more often from confusing curiosity with commitment than from any other single failure. A 5,000-person waitlist costs the signer-up nothing and tells you nothing. Real demand leaves a behavioral fingerprint: someone paid, someone expanded usage, someone built a workflow on top of you, someone scrambled when you broke. If none of that has happened, the demand isn't there yet — no matter how big the waitlist.

## How to apply

- When a founder cites a waitlist, ask: "Has anyone offered to pay before you've shipped?"
- When a founder cites positive feedback, ask: "Has anyone gotten angry when something broke?"
- When a founder cites VC interest, redirect: "Investor enthusiasm is not user pull."
- The strongest demand evidence is panic when something breaks — that means the user has integrated you into their work.

## Examples

1. **Founder:** "We have 500 people on the waitlist." **Push:** "A waitlist is the cheapest commitment possible. Has anyone called you when something broke?"
2. **Founder:** "VCs are excited about the space." **Push:** "VC excitement is about the *space*, not your product. What did a *user* do this week that proves they need you?"
3. **Strong demand evidence:** "A customer called me at 11pm when staging went down for 20 minutes." → Name what was good and pivot to a harder question.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating waitlist size as a leading indicator. Waitlists measure curiosity.
- Letting "that's interesting" stand in for demand. Interest is free; demand costs the user something.
- Accepting investor enthusiasm as user signal. Investors pattern-match on markets, not products.
