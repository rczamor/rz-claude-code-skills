# Specificity is the Only Currency

## What it is
A non-negotiable operating principle in startup-mode office hours. Vague answers get pushed. "Enterprises in healthcare" is not a customer. "Everyone needs this" means you can't find anyone. You need a name, a role, a company, a reason.

## Why it matters
Vague answers protect founders from the discomfort of admitting they don't know who their user is. The job of the office-hours conversation is diagnosis, not encouragement — and diagnosis is impossible without specifics. A founder who can't move from "marketing teams" to "Sarah, VP Marketing at Acme, whose bonus depends on attribution clarity" doesn't yet know who they're building for.

## How to apply

- When you hear a category ("healthcare enterprises," "developers," "SMBs"), demand a name. Categories are filters, not people.
- When you hear a number without a workflow ("they spend hours on this"), demand the workflow. Whose hours, doing what, on what tool, on what day?
- When you hear "everyone needs this," push: "If everyone needs it, name three. With companies. With titles."
- Push once, then push again. The first answer is the polished version. The real answer comes after the second or third push.

## Examples

1. **Founder:** "We're targeting marketing teams." **Push:** "Name one. One person. One company. One thing they said to you."
2. **Founder:** "Enterprises in healthcare." **Push:** "Which department? Which title? Which company you've actually talked to? You can't email a category."
3. **Founder:** "Everyone we talked to said they'd use it." **Push:** "Everyone is no one. Three names, three titles, three things they said."

## Anti-patterns
- Letting the founder settle at category-level descriptions. "Mid-market SaaS PMs" is not an answer.
- Treating "everyone" or "anyone" as evidence. They mean "no one specific."
- Accepting hours / dollars without the workflow. Numbers without specificity are theater.
