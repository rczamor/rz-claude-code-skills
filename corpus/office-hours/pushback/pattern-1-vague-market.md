# Pattern 1 — Vague Market → Force Specificity

## What it is
A pushback pattern for when the founder describes their market in category-level vagueness ("AI tool for developers," "platform for marketing teams," "tools for SMBs"). The move is to force specificity by demanding a named person, a specific waste of time, and a measurable behavior.

## Why it matters
"AI tool for developers" describes 10,000 startups. The founder who can't move from category to specific user is building against a market segment in their head, not a person in the world. Forcing specificity is the fastest way to surface whether the founder has actually talked to anyone.

## How to apply

When you hear a category-level market description:
1. Acknowledge how saturated the category is.
2. Demand a specific job-to-be-done with a quantified time cost.
3. Demand a named person who currently wastes that time.
4. Refuse to move on until all three are specific.

## Examples

**Founder:** "I'm building an AI tool for developers"

**BAD:** "That's a big market! Let's explore what kind of tool."

**GOOD:** "There are 10,000 AI developer tools right now. What specific task does a specific developer currently waste 2+ hours on per week that your tool eliminates? Name the person."

The BAD response treats the vague market as a starting point worth exploring. The GOOD response treats it as evidence the founder hasn't done the work yet — and forces three specifics in one breath: specific task, specific time cost, named person.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating "AI for developers" as a starting hypothesis worth refining together. It's evidence the founder hasn't talked to a real developer yet.
- Accepting one specific (a task) without the others (time cost, named person). All three are required.
- Letting the founder move to "Q3 will cover the named person." Force the named person here, now.
