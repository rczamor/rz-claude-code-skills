# Eric Ries

## What they're known for

Eric Ries is the author of *The Lean Startup* (2011) and *The Startup Way* (2017), and a former CTO at IMVU. His canonical contribution is the Lean Startup methodology — a synthesis of Steve Blank's Customer Development with Toyota's Lean Manufacturing applied to high-uncertainty startup environments. The core ideas: validated learning (the unit of progress is what you learn, not what you ship), the Build-Measure-Learn loop (tighten the cycle to learn faster), the MVP (Minimum Viable Product — the smallest thing that lets you test a hypothesis), and the Pivot vs. Persevere decision (when data says your strategy is wrong, change it). Ries operationalized "lean" as a vocabulary the entire startup ecosystem now speaks.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Lean Startup vocabulary — MVP, pivot, validated learning, B-M-L — is the lingua franca of startup product. Riché doesn't use the canonical 2011 framing as a daily tool, but the underlying concepts are baseline assumptions in every conversation with founders, investors, and PMs. The most load-bearing pieces for Riché's work: (1) Build-Measure-Learn as the model for how AI product cycles work, except compressed from quarters to weeks; (2) Pivot vs. Persevere as the data-driven discipline against emotional persistence; (3) MVP as a concept Riché will continually need to defend against scope creep. Ries also influenced the entire eval-first thinking that's now central to AI PM — his "validated learning" frame applied to AI outputs is exactly what evals do.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "The only way to win is to learn faster than anyone else." (*The Lean Startup*, ch. 1)
- "An MVP is not a smaller version of your product. It's the smallest experiment that lets you validate or invalidate your hypothesis." (*The Lean Startup*, ch. 6)
- "A pivot is a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis. It's not a failure mode. It's a feature of the model." (*The Lean Startup*, ch. 8)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Lean Startup has been over-applied. Marc Andreessen has argued that Lean works for incremental B2B SaaS but produces under-ambitious "small ball" outcomes when applied to category-creating ideas. Peter Thiel makes the same point in *Zero to One* — some products require betting on a non-validated thesis and persisting until the market catches up. The MVP concept has been particularly mangled: in practice, "MVP" often means a half-built v1 that customers actively dislike, which damages trust rather than producing learning. Olsen and Cagan would both say the MVP needs to clear a "Minimum Lovable Product" bar — Ries's original definition was about hypothesis-testing speed, not product quality, and that distinction is consistently lost. For AI products, B-M-L still applies but the "Build" step is compressed (prototyping in minutes) while the "Measure" step is harder (non-deterministic outputs).
