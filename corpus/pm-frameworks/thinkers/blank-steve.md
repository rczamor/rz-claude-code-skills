# Steve Blank

## What they're known for

Steve Blank is a serial entrepreneur (eight startups, four IPOs), Stanford and Berkeley professor, and the originator of the Customer Development methodology — the framework that Eric Ries built Lean Startup on top of. Blank's key books are *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (2005) and *The Startup Owner's Manual* (2012, with Bob Dorf). His canonical contribution is the four-stage Customer Development process: Customer Discovery (find the right customers and problem), Customer Validation (validate they'll pay), Customer Creation (create demand at scale), and Company Building (transition from search to execution). The underlying thesis: a startup is not a smaller version of a company — it's a temporary organization searching for a repeatable, scalable business model. Blank also originated the "get out of the building" mantra and the I-Corps program (NSF-funded entrepreneurship training).

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Blank is foundational reading for the founder mode Riché aspires to. Customer Development is the right framework when Riché is doing zero-to-one product work, founder interviews, or evaluating early-stage companies. The Customer Discovery stage maps closely onto the Torres OST top-of-tree work, but Blank's framing is sharper for pre-PMF environments where the question is not "which opportunity to pursue" but "is there an opportunity at all." The "get out of the building" discipline is the operational counter to the bias-for-building Doshi describes — it's the diagnostic for whether the team has actually talked to potential customers. For Riché's future founder role, the Customer Development sequence is the playbook for the first 12 months.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "There are no facts inside the building. So get the hell outside." (*The Four Steps to the Epiphany*, ch. 1)
- "A startup is a temporary organization in search of a repeatable, scalable business model." (*Startup Owner's Manual*, ch. 1)
- "No business plan survives first contact with customers." (Talks, Stanford E-Corner)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Customer Development is calibrated for hypothesis-driven, founder-led startups — it's harder to apply inside a large company where the "customer" might be an internal stakeholder, or in deep enterprise sales where customers can't articulate needs because they don't yet know the technology exists. The framework also assumes a relatively traditional product — software with a feature set the customer evaluates. For AI products, Customer Discovery is harder because the capability shifts rapidly between conversations (the model improves), and customer feedback is partly about the gap between expectations and AI behavior, which is non-stationary. Critics also note that "get out of the building" can produce false validation — customers say they want things they won't pay for. Blank's framework is rigorous about validation but somewhat thin on the discipline of weighting customer signals.
