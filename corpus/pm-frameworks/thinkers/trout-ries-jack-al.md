# Jack Trout and Al Ries

## What they're known for

Jack Trout (1935–2017) and Al Ries (1926–2022) were marketing strategists who co-authored *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981) and the follow-up *Marketing Warfare* (1986). Their canonical contribution is the concept of Positioning — the idea that strategy is fundamentally about owning a specific word or attribute in the customer's mind, not about features or quality. The customer's mind is finite; only a few brands can occupy each category, and entrenched leaders are extremely hard to displace head-on. Their core advice: if you can't be first in a category, create a new category you can be first in. The book introduced now-canonical concepts like the "ladder" model (mental rankings of brands within a category), the "battle for the mind," and category creation as the dominant strategic move.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Positioning is the load-bearing concept when Riché is doing personal positioning, brand work, or product marketing. The "own a word" framework is the simplest possible positioning test: when a customer encounters your product, what single word do they associate with it? For Riché, the candidate words are "context architecture," "AI product strategy," "consumer intelligence" — and the discipline is that you can only own one. Positioning also explains why incumbent AI products are sticky: customers have already filed them under specific words ("ChatGPT = general purpose," "Claude = thoughtful assistant," "Cursor = AI IDE"), and dislodging those associations is harder than building features. For new AI product launches, the right move is often to create a new category rather than fight for share in an existing one.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Positioning is not what you do to a product. It's what you do to the mind of the prospect." (*Positioning*, ch. 1)
- "If you can't be first in a category, set up a new category you can be first in." (*Positioning*, ch. 6)
- "The basic approach of positioning is not to create something new and different, but to manipulate what's already up there in the mind." (*Positioning*, ch. 1)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Trout and Ries's framework comes from 1970s consumer marketing — durable goods, packaged consumer products, broadcast advertising. The "own a word" mechanic still works but is operationalized differently in 2026: positioning happens through search, social, and AI-summarization channels rather than through TV and print. Critics argue the "ladder" model overstates how rigid mental categories are — modern consumers fluidly recategorize products as use cases evolve. The advice to "create a new category" is also frequently abused as marketing theatre — companies declare a new category that no one else recognizes and then claim leadership of it. For AI products, the Positioning frame is still right, but the speed of category formation is much faster, and the categories are often defined by the AI summarization layer (what does ChatGPT or Perplexity describe your product as?) rather than by the customer's organic mind.
