# Melissa Perri

## What they're known for

Melissa Perri is the author of *Escaping the Build Trap* (2018), the founder of Produx Labs, and the host of the Product Thinking podcast. Her core contribution is the Build Trap concept — when companies measure success by outputs (features shipped) rather than outcomes (problems solved), they get stuck shipping more without moving the needle. The escape is outcome-oriented product management: change the org's measurement system, the team structure, and the operating cadence. She pairs this with the Product Kata (a Toyota-style improvement cycle adapted for product) — understand the direction, analyze current state, set next target condition, choose the step to take.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

The Build Trap is the diagnostic when Riché spots output-over-outcome thinking — at Suzy, in his own work, or in companies he's evaluating. The Product Kata is operationally useful for any improvement loop where you're not yet at the right level of the metric and need a structured way to keep testing your way toward it. Perri's writing on org transformation complements Cagan's *Empowered* / *Transformed* — Cagan describes the destination, Perri describes the change-management path. For Riché's future founder role, *Escaping the Build Trap* is the warning system: how to avoid drifting into output-orientation as the company scales.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "The Build Trap is a death spiral. The more features you ship without measuring outcomes, the more you reinforce the broken loop." (*Escaping the Build Trap*, ch. 1)
- "Outcomes are not the same as goals. An outcome is a measurable change in customer behavior or business performance." (Product Thinking podcast)
- "Strategy is not a document. It's a deployed set of decisions that flows from the company's why down to what each team is working on this quarter." (*Escaping the Build Trap*, ch. 9)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Perri's framing assumes you can negotiate outcome-based goals with leadership — but in many orgs, outputs are what leadership tracks because outputs are what they understand. Doshi would point out that "escaping the build trap" can become a meta-trap: teams who spend so much time arguing about outcome definitions that they ship nothing. The Product Kata is borrowed from Toyota's Improvement Kata and works best for incremental optimization; it's weaker for zero-to-one product work where you don't yet know what good looks like. For AI products, the Build Trap concept maps imperfectly — sometimes shipping more capabilities IS the outcome (because the model is the product), and the trap is instead "eval drift" rather than feature volume.
