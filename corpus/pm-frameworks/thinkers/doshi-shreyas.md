# Shreyas Doshi

## What they're known for

Shreyas Doshi is a former product leader at Stripe, Twitter, Google, and Yahoo who became one of the most-quoted PM voices in the 2020s through Twitter threads, the Lenny's Podcast appearances, and his coaching practice. His canonical contributions cluster around mental models for high-leverage decision-making rather than a single book. The frameworks that get cited most are LNO (Leverage / Neutral / Overhead), Pre-Mortems, the Three Levels of Product Work (Impact / Execution / Optics), High Agency, Opportunity Cost Thinking, and the Seven Biases of Product Teams. His core thesis is that PM impact comes less from output volume and more from concentrating peak energy on the rare 10x-leverage tasks while delegating or eliminating the rest.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Doshi shows up across Riché's working week. LNO is the prioritization default — when Riché is filling time with positive-ROI but not highest-ROI work, the LNO frame surfaces it. Pre-mortems run at PRD finalization and before any major launch. The Three Levels distinction matters because Riché operates as a VP at Suzy where C-level Impact framing is required, but the temptation is to drift into Execution-level optics. Opportunity Cost Thinking — "is this the BEST use of time?" — replaces the weaker "is this a good use?" The Seven Biases (especially McNamara Fallacy and Bias-for-Building) are the standard checklist when reviewing product decisions for blind spots. Doshi's voice on AI products is also influential: he was early on "evals are the new SQL" framing.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Most product decisions don't fail at execution. They fail at the level above execution — at problem selection." (Lenny's Podcast, 2022)
- "LNO is not a productivity framework. It's an attention-allocation framework. The goal isn't to do more. It's to make sure your peak hours go to the 10x stuff." (Twitter thread, 2021)
- "Bias-for-building is the PM disease. We ship before we understand. We confuse motion for progress." (LinkedIn post, 2020)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Doshi's frameworks lean individualist — they're sharpest for senior ICs and lone PMs making personal allocation decisions. He undertheorizes how LNO works in a team where execution speed is itself a leverage activity, or in early-stage startups where the "Neutral" tasks are actually load-bearing because no one else will do them. Cagan would push back on the implicit "PM-as-thinker, engineer-as-executor" framing — Cagan's Empowered Teams model wants the trio doing discovery together, not the PM doing it alone. Torres would add that opportunity-cost thinking only works when you have continuous customer touchpoints to recalibrate against; without that, "best use of time" becomes "best use of my gut feel."
