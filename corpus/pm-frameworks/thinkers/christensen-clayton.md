# Clayton Christensen

## What they're known for

Clayton Christensen (1952–2020) was a Harvard Business School professor and the author of *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997), *The Innovator's Solution* (2003), *Competing Against Luck* (2016), and *How Will You Measure Your Life?* (2012). Two contributions are canonical. First: Disruptive Innovation theory — incumbents lose to entrants who serve the low end of the market with simpler, cheaper offerings that improve over time and eventually eat the high end. Second: Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) — customers don't buy products, they "hire" them to do a job (functional, emotional, social). The classic example: people don't buy a quarter-inch drill; they hire it to make a quarter-inch hole. Better still: they hire the hole to hang a picture. Better still: they hire the picture to feel at home.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

JTBD is the right discovery lens when Riché is interviewing customers, designing surveys, or evaluating value props — especially for AI products where the temptation is to describe capabilities ("our model can do X") rather than the job the user is actually hiring it for. The "milkshake interview" technique (asking when, where, why someone hired the product, what alternatives they considered, what was happening in their life) is a sharper interview pattern than feature-preference questions. Disruptive Innovation is more controversial as applied science but is still useful as a competitive framework — it tells Riché when to worry about an entrant attacking from below (low-cost, simpler) versus a peer attacking head-on. For AI products, the "good enough" threshold question Christensen always asked is now urgent: when does the cheap, broadly capable model become "good enough" to displace the specialized one?

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "People don't buy products. They hire them to do a job." (*Competing Against Luck*, ch. 1)
- "The forces that lead great firms to fail are precisely the forces that made them great." (*The Innovator's Dilemma*, intro)
- "The way you avoid being disrupted is to disrupt yourself." (HBS lecture, repeated across talks)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Christensen's Disruptive Innovation theory has been challenged on empirical grounds. Jill Lepore's *New Yorker* essay (2014) argued that the historical case studies don't hold up to scrutiny — the "disrupted incumbents" often weren't actually disrupted, or recovered. Andy Grove agreed Disruption was real but argued it's only one pattern of many. Critics also note that "low-end disruption" doesn't describe how recent platform shifts (smartphones, cloud, AI) actually unfolded — those were technological substitutions from above, not below. JTBD has held up better but is sometimes over-applied: not every product purchase has a single coherent "job," and the interview technique can produce post-hoc rationalizations rather than honest motivations. For AI products, JTBD is more useful than Disruption, because the dominant strategic question is "what job is the user hiring this AI to do" — not "are we being disrupted from below?"
