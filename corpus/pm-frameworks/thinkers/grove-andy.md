# Andy Grove

## What they're known for

Andrew Grove (1936–2016) was the third employee, third CEO, and longtime Chairman of Intel — the executive who transformed it from a memory-chip maker into the dominant microprocessor company of the 1980s and 1990s. He is the author of *High Output Management* (1983) and *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996), two of the most influential management books in technology. *High Output Management* is essentially the playbook that produced OKRs: Grove formalized the Objectives and Key Results methodology at Intel in the 1970s, which John Doerr later carried to Google. Grove also originated the concept of the Strategic Inflection Point — moments when the fundamentals of an industry shift and incumbents must reinvent or die. His leadership at Intel through the 1985 memory-business exit (a textbook strategic inflection point response) is the canonical case study.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Grove is the meta-thinker — his frameworks underlie much of modern product management without being the trendy reference. *High Output Management* is the right book for any PM moving into management, particularly the chapters on one-on-ones, meetings as managerial output, and the manager-as-leverage concept (a manager's output is the output of their team, not their personal work). The Strategic Inflection Point concept is directly relevant to AI: the entire software industry is at a strategic inflection point right now, and Grove's playbook for navigating one (be paranoid, listen to the data, exit businesses that won't survive, double down on the new direction) is the template. Riché's context-architecture thesis is itself a strategic-inflection-point bet — that the AI transition will reward companies that get context right and punish those that don't.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "The output of a manager is the output of the organizational units under his or her supervision or influence." (*High Output Management*, ch. 1)
- "Only the paranoid survive. The strategic inflection point is when the way a business operates fundamentally changes." (*Only the Paranoid Survive*, intro)
- "If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what would they do? They'd get us out of memories. So why don't we walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves?" (Grove to Gordon Moore, 1985 — quoted in *Only the Paranoid Survive*)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

*High Output Management* was written from the perspective of a hierarchical, hardware-centric organization with relatively stable strategic priorities — its operating cadences (weekly one-on-ones, monthly metrics review) work less cleanly in flat, fast-moving software organizations. Some of the specific recommendations (mandatory weekly one-on-ones, formal staff meetings) are now cargo-culted rather than thoughtfully adapted. Cagan's empowered-teams model implicitly criticizes Grove's manager-led operating system as the prior generation. *Only the Paranoid Survive* is more durable but the Strategic Inflection Point concept can be over-applied — every market shift becomes "an inflection point" in retrospect, which dilutes the analytical sharpness. For AI products, Grove's frameworks still apply, especially on managing through inflection — the discipline of brutal honesty about whether the old playbook still works.
