# Gibson Biddle

## What they're known for

Gibson Biddle is the former VP of Product at Netflix (2005–2010, the era when streaming overtook DVD-by-mail) and former CPO at Chegg. His canonical contribution is the DHM Model: product strategy answers the question "How will your product **D**elight customers in **H**ard-to-copy, **M**argin-enhancing ways?" The three legs are interlocking — delight without hard-to-copy is a temporary win; hard-to-copy without margin is a science project; margin without delight is rent extraction. He pairs DHM with GEM Prioritization (Growth, Engagement, Monetization) and Consumer Science — the hypothesis / experiment / data / conclusion cycle that Netflix institutionalized.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

DHM is the default lens when Riché is doing strategy work. Every product decision — from a feature to a pricing change to a context-architecture choice — gets evaluated three ways: does this delight, is it hard to copy, does it enhance margin? The hard-to-copy leg pulls in Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers as the typology of defensibility (brand, network effects, scale, counter-positioning, unique tech, switching costs, process power). Biddle's specific contribution to AI-product strategy is that "context architecture" is exactly the kind of hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing capability the model rewards — which is why Riché's five-step thesis maps so cleanly onto DHM. GEM Prioritization is the right tool when ranking initiatives against multiple business dimensions (rather than a single north star).

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Strategy is hypotheses with a high probability of working out. Tactics are the bets you make with conviction." (Talk, "Hooked Product Conference," 2019)
- "Most product orgs delight customers. Few do it in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways. That's the difference between a feature and a strategy." (Lenny's Podcast, 2022)
- "Consumer science is not user research. It's the discipline of running experiments to update your beliefs about customers." (Course materials)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Biddle's DHM was developed inside Netflix, a consumer subscription product with massive scale. The "M" in DHM — margin-enhancing — applies cleanly there but is harder to apply in early-stage products where the business model is still in motion. Critics note that DHM rewards thinking about defensibility upstream of customer love, which can produce strategy theatre when the team hasn't yet found PMF. Olsen's PMF Pyramid is sharper for early-stage. Doshi would observe that DHM is a Leverage-tier framework — applying it to every feature is overkill. For AI products, the "hard-to-copy" leg gets more interesting: model weights aren't defensible, but data, distribution, context architecture, and feedback loops are.
