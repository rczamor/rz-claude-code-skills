# Dave Snowden

## What they're known for

Dave Snowden is a Welsh management consultant, the founder of Cognitive Edge and the Cynefin Centre, and the originator of the Cynefin Framework (pronounced kuh-NEV-in, Welsh for "habitat"). Cynefin is a sense-making model that classifies decision contexts into five domains: Clear (formerly Simple/Obvious), Complicated, Complex, Chaotic, and Disorder. Each domain calls for a different decision strategy: Clear contexts use best practices, Complicated contexts use expert analysis, Complex contexts require probe-sense-respond experiments, Chaotic contexts demand immediate action to stabilize, and Disorder is when you don't know which domain you're in. The framework was developed at IBM in the late 1990s and has been adopted across military, government, healthcare, and product strategy contexts.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Cynefin is the right diagnostic when Riché is unsure what kind of decision he's making. AI products are mostly Complex — non-deterministic, emergent, requires probing rather than pre-planning. Most B2B feature work is Complicated — there's a right answer, but it requires expert analysis. Most ops work is Clear — known patterns, known solutions. Misclassifying domains is the meta-mistake: applying Clear-domain rules (best practices, run the playbook) to a Complex situation (AI product launches) produces brittle outputs. The Cynefin / Wardley combo is unusually powerful: Wardley tells you where you are on the map, Cynefin tells you what kind of move makes sense from there.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "If you are running a complex system, retrospective coherence is the best you can hope for. You can't predict outcomes; you can only describe them after the fact." (*Cynefin: Weaving Sense-Making*, ch. 1)
- "Best practice is for the simple domain. In the complex domain, best practice is dangerous because it pretends the system is more predictable than it is." (Talks, Cognitive Edge)
- "We don't have a knowledge-management problem. We have a sense-making problem." (HBR article, 2007)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Cynefin has been criticized for being more rigorous in its description than in its operational guidance — the framework tells you what domain you're in but is sometimes vague about what to do once you've classified. Snowden's writing can also be combative and dismissive of adjacent frameworks (he's particularly hostile to Lean Startup), which limits cross-pollination. For PM work specifically, the framework is high-leverage at the strategic level but can feel like overkill at the feature level — most day-to-day PM decisions don't require Cynefin classification, just LNO. Critics also note that the boundaries between domains are fuzzy in practice, especially between Complicated and Complex, which can make the framework feel more like a vocabulary than a decision aid.
