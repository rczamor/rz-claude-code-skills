# Sachin Rekhi

## What they're known for

Sachin Rekhi is the founder of Notejoy, a former product leader at LinkedIn (where he led Sales Navigator), and a prolific essayist on product strategy via his blog and Lenny's Newsletter guest posts. His canonical contribution is the Product Strategy Stack — a five-layer model that traces every team-level goal back to company mission. The layers, top-down: Company Mission → Company Strategy → Product Strategy → Product Roadmap → Product Goals. Each layer must demonstrably trace to the one above it; if it doesn't, you have strategy drift. The stack is paired with detailed templates for writing each layer.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

The Product Strategy Stack is the right tool when Riché is doing strategic planning at Suzy, evaluating org alignment, or building his future company's operating system. The five-layer trace is a fast diagnostic for whether team-level work is actually executing strategy or just generating motion. It's especially useful for Riché's VP role because the explicit layer separation lets him hold simultaneous conversations with the CEO (mission/strategy layers) and with team leads (roadmap/goals layers) without conflating them. The stack complements Cagan's vision/strategy distinction by adding the lower two layers (roadmap, goals) and the higher one (company mission/strategy), giving a complete picture.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Every product decision should trace to a layer above. If it doesn't, you're drifting." (Blog post, 2018)
- "The most common strategy failure isn't a bad strategy. It's strategy that exists but isn't deployed — written but not lived." (Lenny's Newsletter guest post)
- "A roadmap is not a strategy. A roadmap is the artifact of a strategy already chosen." (Talk, Notejoy)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

The Strategy Stack is structurally clean but can become bureaucratic — it implies that strategy flows top-down, when in practice strategy often emerges from team-level discoveries and bubbles up. Cagan would point out that the model treats the layers as relatively static when in reality the lower layers (roadmap, goals) update much faster than the upper ones, creating strain. For startups, the upper layers (Company Mission, Company Strategy) are often deliberately vague so the company can pivot — Rekhi's framework is calibrated for mid- to large-stage companies. For AI products, the stack works but the Product Strategy layer needs to explicitly include context architecture and evals as strategic decisions, not implementation details.
