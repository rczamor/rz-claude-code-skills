# Taste, Conviction, Influence (the Appreciating PM Skills)

## What it is

As AI handles more of the operational execution work in product development — drafting PRDs, generating mockups, writing tickets, summarizing user feedback — the PM skills that matter most are the ones AI can't yet do well: taste (judgment about what to build and what makes it good), conviction (courage to make bets when the data is incomplete), and influence (ability to align engineers, designers, executives, and customers behind a direction). These are the appreciating skills of the 2020s. Skills that depreciate: ritual coordination, status update writing, retrospective facilitation, ticket grooming, executive-summary writing — all increasingly automated.

## Why it matters

Career trajectories for PMs are bifurcating. PMs whose value was primarily coordination, documentation, and stakeholder management are losing leverage as those activities get automated by AI tools. PMs who develop taste, conviction, and influence become the highest-leverage humans in their org. The pattern is similar to what happened to designers in the 2010s — pure mockup-makers got squeezed; designers with strong product taste and systems thinking thrived. For Riché's career trajectory toward founder, this means the leverage is in deepening taste (what's worth building), conviction (which bets to make), and influence (how to mobilize people). Pure operational excellence is necessary but no longer sufficient. The Mehta competency model needs to weight Strategy and Influence heavier; the Doshi LNO frame increasingly applies — coordination work is Overhead, taste/conviction/influence work is Leverage.

## How to apply

Audit weekly time allocation across taste-conviction-influence work versus operational coordination. Aim to push the ratio toward the appreciating skills — delegate or automate as much of the coordination layer as possible. Develop taste deliberately: study products you find good and bad and articulate why; build a mental library of what good looks like in your domain. Develop conviction deliberately: make bets in writing, with explicit reasoning, before the data is in; review your hit rate. Develop influence deliberately: identify the 5–10 stakeholders whose alignment matters most and invest in those relationships; write public artifacts (memos, posts, talks) that compound your influence over time. None of these are quick wins — they're 5-year skills built through practice.

## Examples

- A senior PM who delegates standup facilitation, sprint coordination, and status updates to AI tools and reallocates that time to customer interviews and prototype iteration
- A PM who writes an opinionated 1-page memo arguing for a controversial architectural decision and uses it to align engineering and exec stakeholders
- A PM who maintains a written "taste journal" — products encountered, what worked, what didn't, why
- A founder who hires for taste in PM interviews (asks candidates to critique a product) more than for execution mechanics

## Anti-patterns

- Confusing seniority with influence — "I'm a VP" is not influence, it's title
- Treating taste as innate rather than built through deliberate practice
- Conviction without data discipline — making bets without acknowledging the data later disconfirms them
- Influence work that's all upward (executive alignment) and none lateral (engineering/design alignment)
- Defending the operational coordination role against automation rather than reallocating up the value chain
