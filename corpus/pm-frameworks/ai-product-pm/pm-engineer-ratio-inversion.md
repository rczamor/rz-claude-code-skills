# PM-to-Engineer Ratio Inversion

## What it is

The PM-to-Engineer ratio is structurally inverting in AI product teams. Traditional product orgs run 1:4 to 1:8 (one PM per four to eight engineers). Andrew Ng publicly described AI teams in 2024 proposing 2:1 ratios — two PMs per engineer. The driver: as AI handles more of the implementation work, the bottleneck shifts from engineering capacity to specification clarity. The constraining resource becomes the cognitive work of defining what to build, what counts as good, and how to evaluate outputs — work that PMs do. Engineers become higher-leverage when given precise specifications and tighter eval rubrics, which means PM throughput, not engineer throughput, governs team velocity.

## Why it matters

If true, this is one of the largest structural shifts in product organization in 30 years. It changes how teams hire, how PMs spend their time, and what counts as a senior PM skill. PMs who built their careers on coordination and stakeholder management may find themselves under-leveraged. PMs who can specify, evaluate, prototype, and synthesize context become the highest-output ICs in the org. For Riché, the implication is that taste, conviction, judgment, and prototyping ability are appreciating skills, while pure execution-and-coordination work is depreciating. The hiring implication: AI-native orgs will hire PMs at higher ratios than traditional orgs; PMs will increasingly be hands-on with prototypes, evals, and context architecture rather than at meetings.

## How to apply

For Riché's role at Suzy and his future founder bets: design AI teams with 1:1 to 2:3 PM:engineer ratios in domains where specification is the bottleneck. Hire PMs who can prototype, write evals, and synthesize context — not just write user stories and run rituals. Resist the temptation to scale engineering ahead of PM throughput; the team will be capacity-bound on the wrong axis. For PM growth and hiring rubrics: weight specification quality, eval design, and prototype literacy as core competencies, not adjacent ones. For external positioning: PMs who can do this work command premium compensation in 2025–2026 hiring markets.

## Examples

- An AI startup running 3 PMs and 5 engineers because PM throughput limits product velocity
- A PM hiring rubric that weights specification clarity and eval design as primary competencies
- A team where every PM ships a working prototype as part of their PRD, replacing static mockups
- A founder hiring two senior PMs before hiring their next two engineers because the bottleneck is on the spec side

## Anti-patterns

- Hiring traditional engineering-heavy ratios in AI orgs and then complaining about specification debt
- Hiring PMs who can't prototype, can't write evals, and can't read code in AI-native teams
- Treating specification work as junior PM work — it's now the most senior work
- Assuming the inversion holds in every AI domain (it's strongest where models can handle most implementation; weaker in domains with heavy custom infrastructure)
