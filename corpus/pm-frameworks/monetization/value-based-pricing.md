# Value-Based Pricing

## What it is
A pricing approach where the price is set based on the *value the customer derives* from the product, not the cost to produce it. Distinct from cost-plus pricing (markup over cost) and competitive pricing (match the market). Requires research into customer willingness-to-pay (WTP), value drivers, and the next-best alternative. Articulated formally by Madhavan Ramanujam in *Monetizing Innovation* (2016).

## Why it matters
Cost-plus pricing leaves money on the table — it ignores the value the customer captures. Competitive pricing locks the product into the market's economics, ceding pricing power. Value-based pricing requires more work but produces the highest-margin economics, especially for products with strong differentiation (Helmer's 7 Powers). The discipline forces the team to articulate value quantitatively, which also shapes positioning, messaging, and product priorities.

## How to apply
1. **Quantify value.** What does one unit of value mean to the customer? Hours saved, errors prevented, revenue generated, costs avoided. Pin a dollar number per unit.
2. **Map willingness-to-pay.** Use Van Westendorp surveys, conjoint analysis, or interview-based pricing exploration to learn the WTP distribution.
3. **Set the price as a fraction of value delivered.** Common range: 10–30% of customer value. Below 10% leaves money on the table; above 30% triggers procurement pushback.
4. **Anchor against the next-best alternative.** "What would the customer pay if they didn't have us?" sets the ceiling.
5. **Re-test as the product matures.** Value delivered grows over product evolution; pricing should track.

## Examples
1. **Salesforce's per-seat-per-month pricing** is loosely value-based — anchored on the per-rep revenue that Salesforce enables, not on the cost to host.
2. **HubSpot tier pricing** is value-based at the tier level — Starter/Pro/Enterprise tiers map to revenue-stage of the customer's business, with prices anchored on each stage's value capture.
3. **Riché's Helm Labs 14x ACV** is a value-based pricing outcome — pricing anchored on the value the buyer was generating from the use case, not the cost to deliver.

## Anti-patterns
1. **Cost-plus disguised as value-based.** Computing cost, adding 30%, calling it "value-based." Misses the methodology entirely.
2. **One-size-fits-all WTP.** Applying a single WTP number across customer segments with different value-capture profiles. Enterprise WTP often 10x SMB WTP for the same product.
