# Connect Around Shared Problems

## What it is
Riché's networking entry point is never a pitch and never a generic compliment — it's a problem the other person is visibly working on, framed through the lens of context architecture. The premise: the most useful relationships in product and AI form when two people independently arrived at the same problem and want to compare notes. Outreach is an invitation to compare notes, not to sell, audition, or extract.

## Why it matters
Pitching invites a yes/no decision. Comparing notes invites a conversation. The shared-problem framing collapses the status gap — a Tier 1 VP and a builder in the trenches can both have a real opinion on "why does context degrade in long agentic loops?" and both come out of the exchange smarter. This is the mechanism that lets Riché bypass the "who are you?" filter that gates most cold outreach.

## How to apply
1. Before reaching out, identify the specific problem the recipient is publicly working on — from posts, talks, products, or job descriptions.
2. Find the angle where Riché's context architecture work intersects that problem. Don't force it; if there's no real overlap, don't reach out.
3. Open the message by stating the problem in their terms, not yours. Then offer the angle Riché has been working on.
4. End with an invitation to compare notes — never a request for time, advice, or introduction.

## Examples
1. To a CPO posting about RAG quality plateaus at Grandstage-adjacent companies: "Your post on RAG plateaus is exactly where I've ended up too — the wins from better retrieval flatten faster than people expect. I think the bottleneck isn't retrieval, it's context shaping. Curious if your team's seen the same."
2. To an AI product lead writing about agent reliability: "The reliability failure modes you described — agents losing the thread mid-task — are the same ones I've been instrumenting. I've started treating context as the failure surface, not the model. Would love to compare what's worked."

## Anti-patterns
- Naming a "shared problem" that's really just a hook for Riché's pitch. The recipient sees it.
- Choosing a problem that's too generic ("AI is hard"). The shared problem must be specific enough that two people who share it would recognize each other.
