# Mutual Value Test

## What it is
Every relationship Riché invests in must pass one test: both parties should be smarter for knowing each other. Networking is career infrastructure built on reciprocal intellectual value, not contact collecting, not LinkedIn-trophy hunting. The unique thing Riché brings is a perspective on context architecture in AI products that other product leaders find genuinely sharpening — not pitch material, not self-promotion.

## Why it matters
The fastest way to look mercenary is to optimize for transaction. People can smell extractive intent in the first DM. When Riché's outreach passes the mutual value test, the response rate goes up, conversations actually start, and the relationships compound — because the other person leaves the exchange with something useful. The opposite — generic "great post" comments and let's-grab-coffee asks with no substance — produces a network that decays the moment Riché stops chasing it.

## How to apply
1. Before sending any message, name what the other person gets from the exchange in one sentence. If the answer is "they get to help me," rewrite.
2. Lead with a specific perspective, frame, or framework — not a question that costs them time to answer.
3. Ask: would I want this exchange if our roles were flipped? If not, sharpen.
4. Track value-given over time per relationship in the CRM. If you're consistently extracting more than offering, recalibrate.

## Examples
1. Cold reach to a Suzy-adjacent VP of Product: instead of "would love to learn from you," send "your team's onboarding flow makes the model uncertainty visible — that's the move most AI products skip. Here's the failure mode I see when teams hide it." That's a perspective they can use immediately.
2. Reconnect with an IBM colleague: rather than "thinking of you," lead with "I've been working on context architecture in AI products and your work on enterprise data governance keeps coming up. Here's why I think the two collide."

## Anti-patterns
- Templated outreach with the recipient's name swapped in. The reader feels the template instantly and discounts the sender.
- Treating "I'll add value later" as sufficient. The first message is the value test, not a promissory note.
