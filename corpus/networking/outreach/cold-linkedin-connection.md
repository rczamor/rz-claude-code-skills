# Cold LinkedIn Connection

## What it is
A first-touch LinkedIn connection request where Riché has no prior relationship with the recipient. Context-first — the message references something specific they wrote, built, or shipped, then connects it to something Riché is working on. No ask in the first message. The connection request itself is the CTA.

## Why it matters
Cold LinkedIn is where most outreach dies. Defaults — "I'd like to add you to my network" — convert at near-zero on senior product leaders. A specific, context-loaded message converts at 30-50% with the right targets, because it self-selects for people who recognize the substance and want to engage. The first message sets the entire trajectory of the relationship.

## How to apply
Structure:
1. One sentence referencing their specific work — a post, talk, product, or shipped feature.
2. One sentence connecting it to something Riché is currently working on or thinking about.
3. One sentence expressing genuine interest in their perspective.
4. No CTA in the first message. Do not ask for time, intro, or feedback.

Length: 250-400 characters. LinkedIn truncates longer messages. Tighter is stronger.

Voice rules: see `corpus/voice/`. No exclamation points. No "amazing." No "would love to learn from your journey."

## Examples

Example message — to a CPO at a Series B AI product company who recently posted about RAG quality plateaus:

> Saw your post on RAG plateaus at the 70% quality mark — I've ended up in the same place. I'm building a personal knowledge engine called Grandstage to prove that context architecture, not retrieval quality, is where the next 20% lives. The five-step gap between data and context is where I'm spending most of my time. Your framing on retrieval ceilings was the sharpest take I've read on this. Would love to follow your work more closely.

Word count: 87. Character count under 500. The message names a specific post, connects to specific Riché work (Grandstage, context architecture, the five-step gap), expresses interest without asking for time.

Example message — to a senior PM at a Forbes AI 50 company who shipped a feature with visible model uncertainty:

> Your team's onboarding flow showing model confidence percentages is the only AI product I've used this year that treats uncertainty as a first-class UX surface. I'm working on a thesis that context architecture, not model intelligence, is the real moat in AI products — and visible uncertainty is one of the hardest pieces to get right. The Hook talk I'm developing leans on this exact pattern. Would love to follow what you ship next.

Word count: 84. Character count under 500. The message references a specific feature (onboarding flow, confidence percentages), names Riché's thesis and a specific project (the Hook), and signals follow rather than ask.

## Anti-patterns
- Including a CTA ("would love to chat sometime") in the first message. It collapses a connection request into a sales touch.
- Generic flattery ("brilliant work!"). Specifics or nothing.
- Mentioning a mutual connection without their permission. Looks like name-dropping; backfires when verified.
