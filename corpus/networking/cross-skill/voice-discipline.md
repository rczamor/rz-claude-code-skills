# Voice Discipline

## What it is
A standing rule: every networking message — DM, email, comment, intro request, follow-up, speaker pitch, advisory response — must be drafted and reviewed against the voice rules in `corpus/voice/`. No off-brand drafts ship, even when the message is "just a quick reply." Voice consistency across surfaces is non-negotiable because the audience encounters Riché across multiple touches and forms a unified impression.

## Why it matters
A LinkedIn post in Riché's voice followed by a DM in someone else's voice (or in default-LinkedIn-voice) breaks the perception of coherence. The audience can't articulate the dissonance; they just feel that something is off. Voice discipline across networking surfaces is what makes Riché feel like one person — same posture in public posts, in DMs, in speaker pitches, in advisory replies. The compounding effect: the audience trusts the consistency, and the trust compounds.

## How to apply

1. **Every draft passes through the voice ruleset before sending.** No exceptions for "low-stakes" messages — the recipient does not know which messages Riché thinks are low-stakes.

2. **Run the Fatal Fifteen check.** Voice has a list of forbidden patterns (per `corpus/voice/`). At minimum, every networking message gets eyeballed against that list before sending.

3. **Apply the read-aloud test.** From `corpus/networking/philosophy/read-aloud-test.md` — every message must pass it. The voice rules and the read-aloud test interlock: voice violations almost always also fail the read-aloud test.

4. **Use the same voice across surfaces.** A speaker pitch and a casual DM have different lengths and registers, but the underlying voice is the same — the cadence, the diction, the lack of filler.

5. **When in doubt, draft in the copywriting skill first.** Don't draft a sensitive message directly in LinkedIn or Gmail; the surface biases toward defaults that violate the voice. Draft in a neutral surface, voice-check, then ship.

## What voice discipline looks like in networking

- **No "Hope this finds you well!"** — Fatal Fifteen violation. Every networking surface is full of this; Riché's isn't.
- **No "I'd love to pick your brain"** — pitch tell, also a voice violation.
- **No exclamation points unless extremely warranted** — networking messages with multiple exclamation points read as eager-junior; Riché's voice is steadier.
- **No em-dash overuse**, no AI-tells, no "diving deep into the weeds" filler — see `corpus/voice/` for the full list.

## Examples
1. A quick LinkedIn reply to a Tier 2 contact's post: drafted in the same voice as a 600-word speaker pitch. Same cadence, same diction, just shorter. The recipient reading both feels they're hearing one person.
2. An advisory inquiry response that the urgent inbox tempted Riché to write fast: held to the voice ruleset anyway, drafted in the copywriting surface, voice-checked, sent. The thirty extra seconds preserve the consistency that took months to build.
