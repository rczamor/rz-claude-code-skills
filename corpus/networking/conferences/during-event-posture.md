# During-Event Posture

## What it is
The behavioral pattern Riché holds during a conference or meetup — how he opens conversations, what he says, how he listens, and how he exchanges contact info. The posture is the operating system that runs while Riché is on the floor; it's what turns the prep work into actual relationships.

## Why it matters
Most networking advice fails because it focuses on what to say rather than how to listen and structure the exchange. The during-event posture is question-led, note-taking, insight-offering, and intent-driven — the four behaviors that consistently produce the kind of conversation the other person remembers and wants to follow up on. The opposite — pitching, monologuing, name-collecting — produces forgettable interactions even when the prep was good.

## How to apply
Four core behaviors:

1. **Lead with questions, not statements.** Open every new conversation with a specific question about their work. Riché's pre-prepared 2-3 questions per target deploy here. Statements (even good ones) close conversations; questions open them.

2. **Take notes on specific things people say** — for follow-up personalization. The note can be a phrase they used, a problem they're working on, a book they referenced. Notes go in the phone or a small notebook between conversations, never during. The follow-up message in 48 hours will reference this specific.

3. **Offer one specific insight per conversation, not a monologue.** When the conversation reaches the right moment, share one frame, one observation, one connection. Not three. The constraint is the discipline — a single useful insight is more memorable than a flood.

4. **Exchange contact info with intent.** "I'd love to send you something about X" — where X is a specific resource, intro, or follow-up tied to what you just discussed. The intent makes the contact exchange purposeful and pre-loads the follow-up. See `corpus/networking/conferences/intent-driven-contact-exchange.md`.

## Examples
1. At ProductTank NYC, Riché opens with the prepared question to a target CPO: "How is your team handling context degradation in long agent loops?" The CPO responds at length about their specific approach. Riché takes a mental note of the phrase "context budgeting" they used. Later, he offers one insight: "the failure mode I see most often is teams instrumenting retrieval quality but not what gets dropped on the floor at the token-limit boundary." Then: "I'd love to send you the writeup I'm doing on that exact instrumentation gap — what's the best way?"

2. At a Lean Product Meetup virtual breakout, Riché stays in his lane: one question about their work, listens, takes a note, offers one frame from his context architecture thesis, and ends with a specific share offer. Three rooms in the breakout produce three follow-ups queued for the next morning.

## Anti-patterns
- Monologuing about Grandstage or the Hook. Even when relevant, more than 30 seconds of Riché-talking feels like a pitch.
- Note-taking during the conversation. Looks transactional. Notes go between conversations.
- Generic contact exchange ("let's connect on LinkedIn"). Without intent, it produces a connection no one acts on.
