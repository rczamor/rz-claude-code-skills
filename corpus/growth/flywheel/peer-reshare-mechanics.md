# Peer Reshare Mechanics

## What it is
The specific things that make a peer (Segment 2 product leader) reshare a post. Not the content tactics most "viral content" advice describes — those produce engagement without reshare. Peer reshares are about *usefulness to the resharer's reputation*. A peer reshares when the post gives them a frame, language, or proof point they want to be associated with.

## Why it matters
Reshares from 1K+ follower accounts are the escape velocity for the echo chamber. Likes don't escape; reshares do. Understanding the mechanics lets you design content that earns peer reshare, not just peer like.

## How to apply
A peer reshares when the post:
- **Names something** they've been thinking but haven't articulated. ("Context architecture is the moat" gives them a phrase.)
- **Builds a frame** they can use with their own team or stakeholders. (A 4-step diagnostic, a 2x2, a clean naming distinction.)
- **Has a credible proof point** that reflects well on them for sharing. (Helm Labs $3.25M pipeline; Suzy launch; specific metrics.)
- **Takes a substantive position** they agree with but couldn't say themselves without political cost. (Direct critiques of common AI PM mistakes.)
- **Has tight, voicy writing.** Voice rules apply universally — see `corpus/voice/`.

## Examples
- Wednesday deep dive on context architecture frames the AI PM challenge as "context fit, not just product-market fit." A senior PM at a Series B company reshares because the phrase is now usable in his own pitches. The reshare hits 4K of his followers.
- Thursday framework post offers a 4-step diagnostic for AI feature prioritization. A VP of Product saves it, then reshares two days later with "wish I'd had this for our last roadmap." The framework gave her something to point her team to.
- A take that says "most AI PM job descriptions miss the context layer entirely" — sharp, specific, with a Helm Labs anecdote — gets reshared because the position is one many peers agree with but won't say themselves.

## Anti-patterns
- Optimizing for likes (questions, polls, "agree?" framings). These produce engagement without reshare.
- Producing safe, agreeable content. Peers reshare positions, not consensus.
