# Engagement Quality vs. Count

## What it is
The discipline rule: optimize for engagement quality, not engagement count. One reshare from a senior PM with 5K followers is worth 50 likes from random connections. One peer DM is worth 200 generic comments. Quality wins, every time.

## Why it matters
The temptation to optimize for count is constant — it's what platform dashboards push, what most "growth advice" advocates, and what produces the cheapest dopamine hits. But count without quality is the echo chamber: more numbers, no actual flywheel motion. Quality is what compounds; count is what flatters.

## How to apply
- **Define quality:** comments from senior+ PMs (Segment 2), reshares from 1K+ accounts, DMs from named professional accounts (not bots, not random connections), engagement from target-list members.
- **Tag every metric with quality.** A "12 comments this week" line doesn't matter. A "12 comments, 3 from senior PMs, 1 from a Lenny-orbit thinker" line does.
- **Refuse count-optimization tactics.** Engagement bait questions ("agree?"), polls without substance, "share if you agree" CTAs — these inflate count and destroy quality.
- **Read the comment threads.** A post with 50 likes and 8 substantive senior-PM comments is healthier than a post with 200 likes and zero comments.

## Examples
- A Wednesday deep dive on context architecture pulls 38 likes (modest) and 11 comments — 4 from senior PMs, 1 from a peer in Lenny's orbit. Quality high; count moderate. The post is doing the right work for the flywheel.
- A "what's your favorite PM framework?" engagement-bait post pulls 200 likes and 80 comments — 90% from junior PMs. Count high; quality near-zero. Post produced no flywheel motion.
- A peer DM from a senior PM at an AI company saying "your last post was the frame I needed for our roadmap conversation" is worth more than the 50 likes that same week.

## Anti-patterns
- Posting engagement bait to inflate count. Burns reputation with Segment 2 for short-term numbers.
- Comparing "this post got X likes" without quality breakdown. The comparison hides the actual signal.
