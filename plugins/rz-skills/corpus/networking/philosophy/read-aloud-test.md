# The Read-Aloud Test

## What it is
A diagnostic for any outreach message: if the recipient read this message out loud to a friend in the next room, would the friend say "this person gets it" or "this person wants something from you"? The test catches messages that pass an internal smell-check but fail in the recipient's social context — which is the only context that matters.

## Why it matters
People share screenshots of bad DMs. Generic, extractive, or self-aggrandizing outreach gets ridiculed in the exact circles Riché is trying to enter. A single read-aloud failure with a Tier 2 contact can poison Tier 1 access through their network. The flip side: messages that pass the read-aloud test get forwarded with a "you should meet this guy" — which is the only inbound channel that consistently produces high-value relationships.

## How to apply
1. After drafting any DM, email, or comment, read it aloud to yourself in the recipient's voice. Imagine them reading it to a peer.
2. Listen for the moment the friend would react. Is it "huh, interesting" or "ugh, another one"?
3. If anything in the message feels like it would embarrass the recipient to share — generic openers, name-drops, asks-disguised-as-curiosity — strip it.
4. Bias toward specificity: a single concrete reference to their work passes; a vague compliment fails.

## Examples
1. Failure: "Loved your recent talk! Would love to connect and learn from your journey." Read aloud, this is the ten-thousandth message they've gotten this week. The friend says: "block them."
2. Pass: "Your point in the talk about treating context as a first-class engineering surface — that's exactly the framing I've been missing in my work on AI product evals. The 'context isn't memory, it's working memory under pressure' line is going to live in my head." Read aloud, the friend says: "wait, who is this?"

## Anti-patterns
- Hiding an ask inside flattery. The friend hears it instantly.
- Optimizing for what sounds humble to the sender vs. what lands as substantive to the recipient. Self-effacing language often reads as needy.
