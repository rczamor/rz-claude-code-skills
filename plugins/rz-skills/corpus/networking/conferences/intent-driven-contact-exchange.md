# Intent-Driven Contact Exchange

## What it is
A specific protocol for exchanging contact info at the close of a conversation: never "let's connect on LinkedIn" alone. Always "I'd love to send you something about X" — where X is a specific resource, intro, or follow-up tied to what was just discussed. The intent transforms the contact exchange from an empty social ritual into a pre-loaded follow-up commitment.

## Why it matters
Default contact exchanges produce LinkedIn connections that no one ever acts on. Both parties forget the conversation within a week, and the "we should chat sometime" sentiment evaporates. Intent-driven exchanges create a specific obligation on Riché's side (to send the thing) and a specific expectation on the other side (to look for it) — which guarantees the next touch happens and gives it a substantive reason to land.

## How to apply
Three rules:

1. **Always close with a specific share offer.** "I'd love to send you the writeup I'm doing on context budgeting — what's the best way?" The X has to be specific and tied to the conversation that just happened.

2. **Use it to disambiguate channel.** Asking "what's the best way to send it?" gets you the email, LinkedIn, or other channel they prefer — better than guessing or defaulting to LinkedIn.

3. **Deliver within 48 hours.** The intent only works if the share actually arrives. Late delivery breaks the trust signal.

Pattern variants:

- **Share offer**: "I'd love to send you [specific writeup, frame, or resource] about [topic just discussed]."
- **Intro offer**: "I know someone working on exactly this problem — would you want me to introduce you?"
- **Follow-up offer**: "I'd love to follow up on [specific thread] — would a quick email work, or are you more on LinkedIn?"

## Examples
1. End of a 10-minute ProductTank conversation about RAG quality plateaus: "I'd love to send you the instrumentation framework I've been building for context-drop visibility — it goes deeper than the post you saw. What's the best way?" Recipient gives email. Riché sends within 24 hours.

2. End of a conference speaker conversation: "Your point about visible uncertainty being trust-rebuilding made me rethink something I'm working on. I'd love to send you the draft of the writeup once I incorporate it — would email or LinkedIn work better?"

3. End of a meetup conversation where the recipient mentioned a problem: "I think Sarah at [company] is wrestling with the exact same thing — do you want me to make an intro?" Intro offer turns the contact exchange into an act of generosity that the recipient remembers.

## Anti-patterns
- Vague intent ("I'll send you something about that"). The "something" needs a name.
- Promising and not delivering. The single highest-cost networking mistake.
