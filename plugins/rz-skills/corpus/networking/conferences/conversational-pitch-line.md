# Conversational Pitch Line

## What it is
The single sentence Riché uses when someone asks "what do you do?" at a conference, meetup, or in any in-person networking moment. It's not the formal speaker hook (which lives in `corpus/growth/speaking/the-hook.md`) — it's the conversational version: a clean, declarative sentence that lands the thesis without sounding rehearsed.

## Why it matters
The "what do you do?" moment is the highest-frequency, highest-leverage micro-interaction in networking. A weak answer ("I'm in product, working on AI stuff") wastes the moment. A strong answer locks in the recipient's attention for the next 30 seconds and makes them ask a follow-up question that opens the real conversation. The line has to be conversational — sounding too rehearsed signals pitch mode and breaks the room.

## How to apply
The canonical line:

> "I'm building a system that proves context architecture matters more than model intelligence in AI products."

Why this works:
- One sentence. One claim. Easy to retain.
- Specific ("context architecture", "model intelligence") — they can't immediately bucket Riché as generic-AI-person.
- Provocative ("matters more than") — invites disagreement, which invites conversation.
- Builder framing ("building a system") — concrete, not theoretical.

How to deploy:
1. When asked "what do you do?", deliver the line. Then stop. Don't elaborate unprompted.
2. The follow-up question they ask becomes the conversation seed. Common follow-ups: "wait, what do you mean by context architecture?" or "more than the model itself? why?"
3. From there, run the during-event posture: question-led, note-taking, insight-offering.

When to vary:
- If the recipient is technical (CPO, engineering leader): the canonical line works as-is.
- If the recipient is non-technical (event organizer, recruiter): "I help product teams figure out what's actually broken in their AI products — usually it's not the model, it's how they're shaping context around it."

## Examples
1. At ProductTank NYC, asked by a fellow PM. Riché: "I'm building a system that proves context architecture matters more than model intelligence in AI products." PM: "Wait, what do you mean? Like prompt engineering?" Riché: "Bigger than that — it's the five-step gap between data and what hits the model. Most products skip those steps. Want me to show you?"
2. At a non-technical event, an event organizer asks. Riché uses the alt version. The organizer follows up with "what kind of products?" Riché names two examples; conversation moves to whether he'd speak at their next event.

## Anti-patterns
- Reciting the line word-for-word with no warmth. Sounds like a memorized commercial.
- Following the line with two more sentences before the recipient responds. The pause is part of the technique.
