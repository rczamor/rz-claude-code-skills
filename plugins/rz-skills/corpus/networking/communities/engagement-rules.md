# Community Engagement Rules

## What it is
Four rules that govern how Riché engages in any product community — Slack, in-person, online forums, conference backchannels. The rules apply universally regardless of community size or curation. They define what makes engagement compound (substantive perspective, real experience, generosity) versus what burns it (link-drops, generic advice, self-promotion-heavy posting).

## Why it matters
Communities have memories. A pattern of low-quality posts builds a low-quality reputation that's difficult to reverse. The rules below produce the opposite — a pattern of presence that makes other members want to follow Riché's work without him asking. The compounding effect is that strong community presence in 2-3 venues produces inbound flow that exceeds what aggressive self-promotion across 10 venues would produce.

## How to apply

The four rules:

1. **Add substantive perspective, don't just promote content.** Every post should advance the conversation. Reference frameworks from `corpus/pm-frameworks/`, share specific Riché experience (Suzy, Grandstage, Helm Labs, IBM), or contribute a real frame. Drop-and-run promotion gets ignored at best, removed at worst.

2. **Never drop a link without adding value first.** If you're going to share a link to your own content, the post needs to deliver standalone value before the link — a frame, a lesson, a specific insight. The link is the supporting reference, not the post.

3. **Answer at least 2 questions for every 1 piece of self-promotion.** The 2:1 ratio is the floor. Stronger ratios (5:1, 10:1) build deeper credibility. Communities track this implicitly; members notice when the ratio inverts.

4. **Reference specific experiences, not general advice.** Generic ("you should think about your customer journey") is worthless. Specific ("at Suzy, we ran into this exact problem when scaling onboarding past 50K monthly users, and what worked was X") is the differentiator. Specifics are what make Riché's perspective valuable.

Bonus pattern: **if someone asks about context, RAG, or AI architecture, that's the opening — take it with substance, not a pitch.** This is Riché's home territory. When the community surfaces a question on his exact thesis, he engages substantively, references Grandstage as a real example only when it helps, and treats the moment as a craft demonstration rather than a sales pitch.

## Examples
1. In MTP #ai-products, someone asks about RAG quality plateaus. Riché's response: a substantive 4-paragraph frame referencing the three-layer context shaping he's been working on, with specifics on what's worked and what hasn't, plus one named example from advisory work. No link to his content. Three days later, multiple DMs ask if he has a writeup — that's when the link goes out.
2. In CPO Club HQ #leadership, someone asks for hiring rubrics for senior PMs. Riché shares the actual rubric he used at Suzy with specific behavioral signals. The post stands on its own; no link, no CTA, no follow-up sale.
3. Counter-example (avoided): seeing a thread about AI evals, posting "I wrote about this here [link]." This is the dropped-link pattern that violates rules 1 and 2 simultaneously.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating community engagement as a content distribution channel. It is the opposite — communities reward people who give value before asking for any.
- Using community DMs to skip the engagement work. Cold DMs from a low-presence member feel extractive even when content is good.
