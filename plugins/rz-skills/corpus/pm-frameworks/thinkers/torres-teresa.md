# Teresa Torres

## What they're known for

Teresa Torres is a product discovery coach and the author of *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021). Her central contribution is the Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) — a visual framework that connects a desired outcome to the customer opportunities (needs, pain points, desires) that could move it, the solutions that could address those opportunities, and the assumption tests that validate the solutions. The tree is paired with Continuous Discovery: weekly customer touchpoints conducted by the product trio (PM, designer, engineer) together, ongoing rather than waterfall. Her work extends Marty Cagan's discovery / delivery distinction with a much more operational toolkit.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Torres's frameworks are the default when Riché is deciding what to build next or evaluating an existing roadmap. The OST forces three things: (1) start with an outcome, not a feature; (2) frame opportunities as customer needs, not solutions; (3) limit WIP — work on one small opportunity at a time. Continuous Discovery — weekly touchpoints by the trio — is the operating system Riché aspires to at Suzy and that he'll likely build into his next company. Torres's "compare and contrast" mindset (rather than "whether or not") is the exact debiasing move when stakeholders ask "should we build X?" Her Product Outcomes vs. Business Outcomes distinction protects teams from being held accountable for metrics they can't directly influence.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Don't ask customers what to build. Ask about behavior." (*Continuous Discovery Habits*, ch. 4)
- "Limit your work in progress. The smaller the opportunity you're working on, the faster you'll learn." (Talk, ProductCon 2020)
- "The product trio is the unit of discovery. Not the PM. The trio." (*Continuous Discovery Habits*, ch. 2)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Torres's model assumes you can run a trio with weekly touchpoints — which is hard at early-stage startups (no designer yet) and at very late-stage enterprise (designer and engineer aren't co-located with PM). The OST is rigorous but can become bureaucratic; Doshi would observe that an over-built OST is itself an Overhead activity. Cagan agrees with Torres on most things but is more emphatic that the PM is the de facto leader of the trio — Torres flattens the hierarchy more. For AI products, Torres's framework needs adaptation: "behavior" is harder to observe when the product produces non-deterministic outputs, so the assumption-test branch of the OST often becomes an evals exercise rather than a usability test.
