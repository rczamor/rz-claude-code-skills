# W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne

## What they're known for

W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne are INSEAD professors and the co-authors of *Blue Ocean Strategy* (2005, expanded edition 2015) and *Blue Ocean Shift* (2017). Their canonical contribution is the Red Ocean / Blue Ocean distinction: Red Oceans are existing markets where competitors fight for known customers and shrinking margins; Blue Oceans are uncontested market spaces created by reframing the problem. The methodology is the Four Actions Framework — Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create — applied to the dimensions on which an industry currently competes. The most famous case study is Cirque du Soleil, which created a new category by eliminating animal acts, reducing star performers, raising venue elegance, and creating thematic narratives. Other examples: Wii (gaming for non-gamers), Yellow Tail (wine for non-wine-drinkers), Southwest (point-to-point flying).

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Blue Ocean is the right tool when Riché is evaluating whether a product strategy is genuinely category-creating or just incremental. The Four Actions Framework — Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create — forces explicit decisions about what NOT to do, which is the heart of strategy (per Porter). For AI product positioning, Blue Ocean is unusually relevant because the temptation is to compete on the same dimensions as established AI products (model quality, speed, price) when the larger opportunity is to create a new category by reframing the job. Riché's context-architecture thesis is itself a Blue Ocean move — instead of competing on model quality (Red Ocean), it reframes the problem as context quality, which most competitors don't yet contest.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Blue ocean strategy is about competing in uncontested market space and making the competition irrelevant." (*Blue Ocean Strategy*, ch. 1)
- "Value innovation is the cornerstone of blue ocean strategy. It's the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation and low cost." (*Blue Ocean Strategy*, ch. 2)
- "Don't ask how to beat the competition. Ask how to make the competition irrelevant." (Talks, INSEAD)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Blue Ocean has been criticized for survivorship bias — the case studies are post-hoc explanations of successes, with little evidence that the framework helps create those successes prospectively. Many "Blue Oceans" turn out to be small markets that don't justify the investment, or they get rapidly invaded once proven. Porter would argue that the strategic distinction Blue Ocean is making (find unconquested space) is actually well-handled by his Generic Strategies typology under "differentiation focus" — Blue Ocean is just a brand. The Four Actions framework is more useful as a creativity prompt than as a rigorous analytical tool. For AI products specifically, the "uncontested" claim is hard to maintain for long — every Blue Ocean quickly becomes Red as the foundation model layer commoditizes downstream.
