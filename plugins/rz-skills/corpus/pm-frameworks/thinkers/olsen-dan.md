# Dan Olsen

## What they're known for

Dan Olsen is a product management consultant, conference speaker, and the author of *The Lean Product Playbook* (2015). His canonical contribution is the Product-Market Fit Pyramid — a five-layer framework that decomposes PMF into testable components. Working bottom-up: Target Customer → Underserved Needs → Value Proposition → Feature Set → UX. Each layer constrains the layers above it: you can't define a value prop without underserved needs; you can't design a feature set without a value prop. The pyramid is paired with the Lean Product Process — a six-step iterative cycle for finding PMF.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

The PMF Pyramid is the right tool when Riché is evaluating whether a product concept has PMF potential — for Suzy initiatives, for his own founder bets, or for companies he's diligencing. It's also the diagnostic when something isn't working: PMF problems usually trace to a layer below the one you're working on (e.g., the UX is fine, but the value prop is unclear). The bottom-up structure forces clarity about who the target customer is before discussing features — the inverse of how most product debates start. For AI products, Olsen's pyramid still applies but the "Feature Set" layer collapses somewhat (the model is the feature) while the "Underserved Needs" layer becomes more critical because AI capabilities outpace clearly defined needs.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Product-market fit is not a moment. It's a destination you approach asymptotically." (*The Lean Product Playbook*, ch. 1)
- "Most products fail at the underserved-needs layer. The team built something well — but for needs that weren't actually underserved." (Talk, Lean Product Conference)
- "If you can't articulate your target customer in one sentence, you don't have one." (*The Lean Product Playbook*, ch. 3)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

The PMF Pyramid is a clean conceptual model but can feel mechanical — real PMF emerges from a messier process than the bottom-up construction implies. Sean Ellis's PMF survey ("how would you feel if you could no longer use this product?") is empirically richer than Olsen's layer-by-layer assessment. Cagan would push back on the implicit waterfall in the pyramid (define customer, then needs, then value prop) — in reality, all five layers iterate together. Torres's Continuous Discovery is more honest about the back-and-forth. For AI products, the pyramid struggles because the underserved needs you're addressing may not pre-exist in the customer's articulated language — they emerge through use of the product.
