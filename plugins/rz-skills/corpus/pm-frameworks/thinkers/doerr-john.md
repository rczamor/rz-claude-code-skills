# John Doerr

## What they're known for

John Doerr is a venture capitalist at Kleiner Perkins (where he led the Google and Amazon investments) and the author of *Measure What Matters* (2018) and *Speed and Scale* (2021). His canonical contribution is popularizing OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) as the goal-setting system for high-performance organizations. Doerr learned OKRs at Intel from Andy Grove in the 1970s and brought the framework to Google in 1999, where it became the operating system that scaled the company from a hundred people to hundreds of thousands. The framework: Objectives are inspirational, qualitative, time-bound; Key Results are measurable, quantitative outcomes that prove the Objective is being achieved. OKRs cascade through the org but are not used for compensation (Grove's original insistence) — they're a focusing tool, not a performance management tool.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

OKRs are the dominant goal-setting system in product companies — Riché has used them at Suzy and will use them at his next company. Doerr's framing is the operational version that most modern teams adopt, more popularized than Grove's original. The right time to invoke Doerr-style OKRs: at quarterly planning, when negotiating outcomes with leadership, when team objectives are diffuse and need sharpening into measurable form. The most important Doerr-specific point: don't tie OKRs to compensation. The moment OKRs become bonus drivers, teams negotiate sandbagged targets and the focus mechanism breaks. Riché has likely encountered this anti-pattern and should defend against it. The "Speed and Scale" framework — applying OKRs to climate problems — is a useful template for any large-scale, multi-stakeholder problem.

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

OKRs as Doerr describes them work well in mature, well-managed companies where the strategy is reasonably clear. They work poorly in early-stage startups where the strategy is still in motion — quarterly OKRs imply more stability than pre-PMF startups have. Many companies adopt OKRs as theatre: they write them, file them, and never refer to them again, which is worse than not having them. Critics note that OKRs can create local optimization at the expense of cross-team coordination — teams hit their KRs while the company misses its goals. Andy Grove's original framing in *High Output Management* is sharper on the "no compensation tie" point and on the discipline of grading OKRs at end of period. For AI products, OKRs work but the quarterly cadence is too slow — many AI product teams now run monthly or bi-weekly OKR cycles to match the pace of model and capability change.
