# Hamilton Helmer

## What they're known for

Hamilton Helmer is the co-founder of Strategy Capital, an investment firm, and the author of *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy* (2016). His canonical contribution is the 7 Powers framework — a typology of seven distinct sources of durable competitive advantage (what he calls "Power"): Scale Economies (per-unit cost decreases with volume), Network Economies (value increases with users), Counter-Positioning (incumbents can't copy without damaging existing business), Switching Costs (customers face friction to leave), Branding (durable trust premium), Cornered Resource (privileged access to a scarce asset), and Process Power (embedded operational know-how). The framework is the strategic answer to "what makes this defensible?" and is widely used in venture capital and corporate strategy. Helmer developed it while teaching at Stanford GSB and applies it to investing.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

7 Powers is the right tool when Riché is evaluating defensibility — for Suzy, for his founder bets, for diligence on companies he's networking with. The seven categories are exhaustive enough to surface defensibility patterns that aren't obvious; the discipline of asking "which of the seven powers am I actually building?" is a sharp diagnostic. For AI products specifically, the framework reveals why most AI companies don't yet have durable Power: foundation models are commoditizing (no Cornered Resource), there are weak network effects (no Network Economies), brands are still forming (limited Branding), and switching costs are low. The strongest AI defensibility patterns map to Process Power (embedded eval and context know-how), Counter-Positioning (incumbents can't move without cannibalizing), and rarely Cornered Resource (proprietary data / distribution). Riché's context-architecture thesis is itself a Process Power play.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Power is the conditions for durable differential returns. Strategy is the path to creating Power." (*7 Powers*, ch. 1)
- "Without Power, competition will erode any returns you generate. With Power, competition is contained." (*7 Powers*, ch. 2)
- "Counter-Positioning is the most underappreciated Power. It defeats incumbents whose existing economics block their response." (Stanford GSB lectures)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

7 Powers is rigorous but high-level — the framework tells you what category of Power you're building but not how to build it. The diagnostic value is high; the operational value requires translation into actual product decisions. Critics note that Helmer's case studies skew toward late-stage public companies (Adobe, Netflix, Microsoft) and the framework is harder to apply to early-stage where Power is anticipated rather than realized. Christensen would push back that "Counter-Positioning" is just his Disruption thesis renamed. For AI products, 7 Powers is unusually relevant because the question of which Powers will form is genuinely open — the answer determines which AI companies are durable and which are temporary winners. Network effects in AI are weaker than in social or marketplace products, which means most defensibility will come from Process Power and Cornered Resource patterns.
