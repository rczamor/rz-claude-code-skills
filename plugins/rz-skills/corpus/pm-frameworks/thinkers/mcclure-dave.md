# Dave McClure

## What they're known for

Dave McClure is a serial investor, former PayPal employee, and the founder of 500 Startups (now 500 Global), one of the early seed-stage accelerators. His canonical contribution is the AARRR Funnel — Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral — also known as Pirate Metrics (because the acronym pronounced as "AAARRR" sounds like a pirate). The framework decomposes the customer lifecycle into five measurable stages, with conversion rates between each stage that can be optimized independently. McClure introduced the framework around 2007 and it became the dominant operating model for early-stage growth metrics in the 2010s. He also originated influential thinking on startup metrics, founder economics, and the case for international startup accelerators.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

AARRR is the right framework when Riché is setting up metrics for a new product, evaluating a startup's funnel health, or doing growth diagnostics on Suzy or his own ventures. The five-stage decomposition forces explicit measurement at each stage rather than just looking at top-line revenue. The framework is most useful for early-stage products where the team needs to know which stage is the bottleneck — fixing acquisition when retention is broken just adds users to a leaky bucket. For AI products, AARRR still works but the Activation stage is unusually critical: an AI product that doesn't deliver perceived value in the first three sessions has likely failed activation regardless of acquisition. The framework also pairs naturally with cohort analysis — AARRR by cohort reveals whether the funnel is improving over time.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Most startups don't have a metrics problem. They have a 'which metric matters' problem. AARRR makes you decide." (Talks, Web 2.0 Expo, 2007)
- "Acquisition without activation is vanity. Activation without retention is leak. Retention without revenue is charity." (Slide deck, 500 Startups)
- "Pirate Metrics doesn't tell you what to do. It tells you what to measure so you know what to do." (Blog post, 2010)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

AARRR has been criticized for treating the lifecycle as linear when modern products often have multiple parallel funnels (e.g., a product with both PLG and sales-led motions has two AARRR funnels). The framework also undertheorizes the Referral stage — viral growth dynamics are richer than a single conversion rate captures, which is why Andrew Chen's loop framework largely supersedes AARRR for late-stage thinking. Sean Ellis would observe that AARRR is a lagging measurement system, not a strategy — it tells you what's broken but not what to do. Critics note that Acquisition and Activation can be hard to cleanly separate in practice (when does "trying the product" become "activated"?). For AI products, AARRR still applies but Retention is the dominant metric: most other stages are easy compared to building durable retention in a non-deterministic product.
