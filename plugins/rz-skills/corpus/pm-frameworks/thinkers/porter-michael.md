# Michael Porter

## What they're known for

Michael Porter is a Harvard Business School professor and the most-cited author in the field of competitive strategy. His canonical contributions are *Competitive Strategy* (1980), *Competitive Advantage* (1985), and the Five Forces framework — the analytical model that describes industry attractiveness as a function of five competitive forces: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of buyers, bargaining power of suppliers, threat of substitute products, and rivalry among existing competitors. Porter also originated the Generic Strategies typology (cost leadership, differentiation, focus) and the Value Chain analysis. His work essentially founded the modern academic field of business strategy and is the implicit foundation underneath much of subsequent strategic thinking.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Porter is essential vocabulary even though his frameworks are 40+ years old and have known limits. Five Forces is the right tool when Riché is evaluating an industry he's considering entering — for his founder bets, for new Suzy verticals, or for diligence on companies he's networking with. The framework forces explicit assessment of structural industry economics rather than letting the team default to "the market is huge" hand-waving. Generic Strategies (cost leadership vs. differentiation vs. focus) is the simplest first-pass strategic positioning question. Value Chain analysis is useful when decomposing where value is captured in a product — particularly for AI products where the value chain is being rapidly restructured (compute → models → context → applications).

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "The essence of strategy is choosing what NOT to do." (HBR article: "What Is Strategy?", 1996)
- "Operational effectiveness is not strategy." (HBR, 1996)
- "The strongest competitive forces determine the profitability of an industry and become the most important to strategy formulation." (*Competitive Strategy*, ch. 1)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Porter's framework was developed during an era of slow industry change and capital-intensive incumbents. The Five Forces model treats industry boundaries as relatively fixed — but in tech, industries blur and reshape rapidly (is Substack a media company, a SaaS company, or a marketplace?). Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers is a more contemporary update that fits better with modern tech business models. Wardley would argue Porter is operating at the wrong granularity — strategy in modern tech requires understanding where individual components are on the evolution axis, not just industry-level forces. Christensen's Disruption theory was partly a critique of Porter, arguing that Five Forces missed the dynamic by which industries get attacked from below. For AI products, Five Forces still works at the industry level, but the bargaining power of suppliers (foundation model providers) is an unusually fast-moving variable.
