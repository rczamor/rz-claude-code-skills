# Sean Ellis

## What they're known for

Sean Ellis is the original "growth hacker" — he coined the term in 2010 — and the founder of GrowthHackers.com and Hacking Growth (consultancy). He led growth at Dropbox, Eventbrite, LogMeIn, and several other now-canonical examples of viral consumer growth in the early 2010s. His most-cited contributions: the Sean Ellis Test for product-market fit ("How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" — answers cluster as Very Disappointed / Somewhat Disappointed / Not Disappointed; PMF is reached when 40%+ are Very Disappointed); the ICE prioritization framework (Impact × Confidence × Ease); and operational frameworks for high-tempo growth experimentation. He co-authored *Hacking Growth* (2017) with Morgan Brown.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

The Sean Ellis Test is the most empirically grounded PMF measurement available. Riché should run it whenever evaluating whether a product (his own, Suzy's, or one he's diligencing) has reached PMF — it's stronger than the Olsen pyramid alone because it operationalizes the destination as a survey result. ICE is the right prioritization tool when speed matters more than rigor (RICE is rigorous but slower). For Riché's growth-marketing work (the rz-growth-marketing skill), Ellis's writing on growth experiments, north-star metrics, and growth loops is foundational vocabulary even when the specific tactics are dated. Ellis's GrowthHackers community essentially professionalized the role of growth PM as distinct from acquisition marketing.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "If you can get 40% of your users to say they would be very disappointed without your product, you have product-market fit." (Blog post, 2009)
- "Growth hacking is not a marketing role. It's a product role with a marketing distribution function." (Talks, 2011)
- "Speed beats elegance. The fastest team to learn wins." (*Hacking Growth*, ch. 3)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

The Sean Ellis Test has been criticized as overly broad — the 40% threshold is empirically derived from a small sample and may not generalize. Modern growth practitioners argue that net retention and engagement curves are stronger PMF signals than survey response. Andrew Chen would push back that the "growth hacking" frame oversells short-term tactics relative to durable loop and network mechanics. ICE has been largely replaced by RICE in modern PM practice because the "Reach" addition is genuinely useful. The growth-hacking era of 2010–2015 produced many tactics that don't work today — most viral mechanics from that era are now blocked by platforms or saturated. For AI products, Ellis's frameworks still apply, but PMF in AI is non-stationary — what's PMF this quarter may not be PMF next quarter as customer expectations shift.
