# Jeff Patton

## What they're known for

Jeff Patton is a product management consultant and the author of *User Story Mapping* (2014). His canonical contributions are User Story Mapping — a visual technique for organizing user stories along the user's journey horizontally and prioritizing depth vertically — and the operationalization of Dual-Track Agile, the practice of running discovery and delivery as parallel concurrent tracks rather than waterfall stages. Patton was an early Agile signatory and one of the strongest critics of "feature factory" Agile that ships outputs without learning. Story Mapping became canonical in Agile PM circles in the mid-2010s and is still the dominant technique for organizing roadmap-level work into user-facing flows.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Story Mapping is the right tool when Riché is breaking down a complex feature into shippable slices, particularly when the feature has multiple user steps and the team needs to decide which steps to build deeply vs. shallowly. The horizontal axis (user journey, left to right) forces explicit sequencing, and the vertical axis (priority, top to bottom) forces explicit MVP definition. Dual-Track Agile is the operating cadence Riché aspires to at Suzy and would build into his future company — discovery work happening one or two sprints ahead of delivery work, with sync at sprint boundaries. Patton's framing is sharper than Cagan's discovery / delivery distinction at the operational level: he describes how the two tracks actually intersect during planning ceremonies.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "User Story Mapping is the antidote to flat backlogs that obscure what the product actually does." (*User Story Mapping*, ch. 1)
- "If you build a feature without understanding the journey it sits inside, you're building a feature factory." (Talk, ProductCon)
- "Dual-track is not two teams. It's one team with two cadences." (Blog post, 2008)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

User Story Mapping is rigorous but can be heavy — for small features, the mapping exercise costs more than it returns. The technique is best for medium-to-large initiatives where multiple user steps and prioritization choices need to be made visible. Dual-Track Agile assumes the team has the headcount to actually run two tracks concurrently — many startup teams have one PM, one designer, three engineers, and the "discovery track" is whatever the PM does between standups. Cagan's version of dual-track is more honest about the team-size requirements. For AI products, Story Mapping struggles because the user journey is more emergent — the user discovers what the AI can do through use, so a pre-planned story map can miss the actual journey. Patton's dual-track is still relevant but the discovery track in AI products is heavily dominated by evals work rather than usability research.
