# Marty Cagan

## What they're known for

Marty Cagan is the founder of Silicon Valley Product Group (SVPG) and the author of three foundational books: *Inspired* (2008/2017), *Empowered* (2020), and *Transformed* (2024). His core contribution is the Product Operating Model — 20 principles for how high-performing product organizations run, anchored on four ideas: empowerment (give teams problems, not features), outcomes over output, sense of ownership, and collaboration. His Empowered vs. Feature Teams distinction is now PM canon: feature teams take orders and ship features; empowered teams are given problems and discover solutions. The Discovery / Delivery split — concurrent, not sequential — is the operating model that most modern PM practice descends from.

## Why they matter to a Riché-facing skill

Cagan is the load-bearing framework when Riché is structuring teams, writing about org dynamics, or evaluating whether Suzy is operating as feature teams or empowered teams. The Product Vision (years) / Product Strategy (quarterly) cadence is the structure for strategic planning. The Trust Barrier — "the largest barrier to empowerment is trust" — is the diagnostic for why transformations stall, and it's directly relevant to Riché's executive influence work. For Riché's future founder role, *Empowered* is the playbook for how to set up the org from day one. The Discovery / Delivery distinction undergirds the Torres OST work — Cagan provides the structural model, Torres provides the discovery toolkit.

## Quotable / signature ideas

- "Feature teams are mercenaries. Empowered teams are missionaries." (*Empowered*, ch. 3)
- "The job of a product team is to solve problems for our customers and our business in ways that are valuable, usable, feasible, and viable." (*Inspired*, ch. 2)
- "Transformation is not about adopting new processes. It's about earning trust." (*Transformed*, ch. 1)

## Where they're wrong / disagree with others

Cagan's model is calibrated for product-led B2C and prosumer companies (Netflix, Adobe, Spotify) — the reference set in his books. It's harder to apply cleanly in deep enterprise sales motions, hardware, or regulated industries where "discovery" looks different and the trio model doesn't always work. Critics argue Cagan idealizes the empowered team to the point of underweighting how rarely the conditions for it exist — most companies will never have the executive maturity, customer access, and engineering autonomy his model assumes. Perri's *Escape the Build Trap* covers the same ground from the org-design side and is sharper on what to do when the trust isn't there yet. For AI products, Cagan's model still applies, but the "feasible" leg of the four risks (valuable / usable / feasible / viable) becomes more dynamic — feasibility shifts as models improve.
