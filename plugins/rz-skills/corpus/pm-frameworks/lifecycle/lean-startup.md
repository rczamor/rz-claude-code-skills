# Lean Startup

## What it is
A management methodology coined by Eric Ries in *The Lean Startup* (2011), drawing on Steve Blank's customer development and Toyota's lean manufacturing. The core mechanic: treat every product as an experiment under conditions of extreme uncertainty. Define a hypothesis, build the smallest test (MVP), measure with actionable metrics, and learn — then either pivot (change direction) or persevere (keep iterating). The unit of progress is *validated learning*, not features shipped.

## Why it matters
From Ries, *The Lean Startup* (2011). Lean Startup re-frames the failure mode of most new products: building things customers don't want, in stealth, until you run out of money. Replacing "are we executing the plan?" with "are we learning faster than we burn?" forces a different operating cadence — one that the rest of PM canon (continuous discovery, dual-track agile, OKRs tied to behavior) inherits.

## How to apply
1. State the leap-of-faith hypotheses for the business — value hypothesis (will users want this?) and growth hypothesis (will it scale?).
2. Design the smallest experiment that can falsify each. The MVP is a means to validated learning, not a v1.
3. Instrument actionable, accessible, auditable metrics. No vanity metrics (downloads, registered users with no behavior).
4. After each cycle, decide pivot vs. persevere. Pivots: zoom-in, zoom-out, customer-segment, customer-need, platform, business architecture, value capture, engine of growth, channel, technology.
5. Run innovation accounting against learning milestones — baseline → tuning → pivot/persevere — not financial projections.

## Examples
1. **Dropbox MVP video.** Drew Houston shot a 3-minute screen recording demonstrating syncing before the product worked at scale. Beta waitlist jumped from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight — validated demand without building the product.
2. **IMVU pivot.** Ries's own startup launched as a 3D-avatar IM add-on. Users wouldn't add the IM client. The pivot: standalone 3D chat. Same tech, different business architecture.
3. **Riché's Grandstage 300% growth at $0 CAC** mirrored this loop: start narrow with a single underserved buyer, instrument behavioral signal, expand only after validated learning, never before.

## Anti-patterns
1. **MVP as v1.** Teams ship a minimal *product* and call it the MVP, but never define the hypothesis it tests. The result is a half-finished product, not a learning artifact.
2. **Pivot fatigue.** Treating "pivot" as motion rather than a falsified-hypothesis decision. Real pivots are rare, deliberate, and traceable to a specific failed test.
