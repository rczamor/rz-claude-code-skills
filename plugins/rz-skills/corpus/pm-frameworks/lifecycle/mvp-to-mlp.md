# MVP to MLP

## What it is
A staged release model. **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** — Eric Ries's term — is the smallest artifact that tests the core hypothesis. **MLP (Minimum Lovable Product)** — popularized by Brian de Haaff (Aha!) and Jiaona Zhang — adds early delight: the smallest version that customers will actually love and recommend, not just tolerate. The path: MVP validates whether anyone wants this; MLP validates whether they'll keep using and refer it; full product industrializes the loved experience.

## Why it matters
MVP alone has a known failure mode: customers tolerate a clunky MVP for the duration of a closed beta, then churn at scale because the product never got past "viable." MLP forces a second checkpoint: have we crossed from utility to affection? This matters most in consumer and PLG categories where word-of-mouth and retention drive economics — viable is not enough when CAC depends on referral.

## How to apply
1. **MVP scope.** Cut everything that isn't the core hypothesis test. Use Ries's actionable-metric criterion: can this artifact falsify the hypothesis?
2. **MLP scope.** Add the two or three things that turn tolerance into delight — usually micro-interactions, voice, or one moment of magic. Reference Kano's "delighters."
3. **Don't conflate.** MLP is not "MVP plus polish." Polish on a wrong product is wasted. Validate the MVP hypothesis first.
4. **Measure the gap.** Between MVP and MLP, instrument NPS, retention curves, and referral. The MLP threshold: enough users would be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared (Sean Ellis's PMF survey).
5. **Industrialize at MLP.** Only after the loved version is identified should the team scale infrastructure, support, and GTM.

## Examples
1. **Superhuman** explicitly built to MLP, not MVP — Rahul Vohra's PMF process delayed launch for years until 40% of users said they'd be "very disappointed" without it. The product was "lovable" before it was "scaled."
2. **Slack's MVP** was a tool for Tiny Speck's internal team. The MLP shift came when external teams adopted it and the team added the playful tone, slash commands, and integrations that drove the love.
3. **Riché's Helm Labs $3.25M pre-launch pipeline** is closer to "MLP-validated demand" than MVP — the buyer-side commitment indicates they would be very disappointed without the product, before code shipped.

## Anti-patterns
1. **MVP as v1.** Shipping a barely-working full product instead of a focused experiment. Customers churn before the team can learn anything.
2. **Skipping MVP for MLP.** Investing in delight before validating utility. Loved products without users still fail.
