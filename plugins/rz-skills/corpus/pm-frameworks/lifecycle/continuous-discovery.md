# Continuous Discovery

## What it is
Teresa Torres's lifecycle pattern, from *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021). Defined verbatim in the product-management skill: "Weekly customer touchpoints by the team building the product. Product trio (PM, designer, engineer) does discovery together. Interviews discover opportunities, not validate solutions. Don't ask customers what to build; ask about behavior." The mechanic: discovery is not a phase that ends; it is a habit that runs in parallel with delivery, every week, owned by the trio.

## Why it matters
Replaces the failure mode where research is a quarterly project, findings arrive after specs are locked, and the team builds against stale insight. By making discovery weekly and trio-owned, the team's mental model of the customer is always within seven days of the customer's actual behavior — closing the gap that produces "we built the wrong thing" post-mortems.

## How to apply
1. **Weekly touchpoints.** Minimum one customer interview per week, every week. Not project-based.
2. **Trio ownership.** PM, designer, engineer all participate. No "researcher hands findings to PM" handoff.
3. **Behavioral interviews.** Ask about specific past instances ("walk me through the last time you..."), not opinions ("would you use...").
4. **Continuous synthesis.** Interview output feeds the opportunity solution tree (OST) — opportunities surface, get prioritized, get tested.
5. **Pair with delivery.** Discovery runs alongside delivery, not before it. Weekly opportunities trigger weekly assumption tests, which feed the next sprint.

## Examples
1. **Suzy Decision Engine** development depended on continuous touchpoints with insights leaders across 350+ enterprise customers — every week revealed friction in the insights → action chain that the trio could test against.
2. **Riché's Grandstage** operated continuous discovery before the term existed — direct buyer conversations every week drove the 300% growth at $0 CAC.
3. **Intercom's Jobs-to-Be-Done practice** combines continuous discovery with JTBD — every week the trio interviews customers about specific switching events, then maps to the OST.

## Anti-patterns
1. **Outsourced discovery.** Hiring a research function to do interviews "for" the trio. Defeats the model — discovery is a habit of the people who decide what to build.
2. **Solution validation.** Showing customers a mock and asking "would you use this?" Customers cannot reliably forecast their own behavior. Ask about past behavior instead.
