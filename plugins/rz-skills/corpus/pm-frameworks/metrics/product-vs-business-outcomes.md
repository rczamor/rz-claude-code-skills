# Product vs. Business Outcomes

## What it is
Teresa Torres's distinction, quoted verbatim from the product-management skill: "Teams should be assigned product outcomes they can directly influence, not business outcomes they can't. Negotiate outcomes with leadership. Translate business metrics into behavioral product metrics." A **business outcome** is something leadership cares about (revenue, NRR, LTV); a **product outcome** is the customer behavior the team can change with product moves (activation rate, weekly retention, feature adoption) that drives the business outcome upstream.

## Why it matters
A team that owns a business outcome it can't move is set up to fail. Marketing, sales motion, pricing, and competitive forces all influence revenue — the product team can move only a subset. Assigning product outcomes is honest: it gives the team a target it can actually hit, then makes leadership responsible for the leap from product outcome to business outcome (pricing, GTM, etc.).

## How to apply
1. **Start from the business outcome.** Leadership wants NRR ≥ 120%, or revenue +30% YoY, or churn ≤ 5%.
2. **Identify the user behaviors that drive it.** What does a customer do that, repeated, produces NRR ≥ 120%? Probably: increased seat usage, new feature adoption, expanded workspace count.
3. **Assign the team the behavioral product outcome.** "Increase weekly active seats per account from 8 to 14."
4. **Negotiate.** If leadership pushes back ("you should own NRR"), explain that the team can move seat usage, not pricing or sales-motion variables. Offer accountability for what they control.
5. **Re-check the linkage quarterly.** If product outcome moved but business outcome didn't, the upstream model is broken — surface and fix at the leadership level.

## Examples
1. **Riché at Suzy** — Decision Engine team likely owns product outcomes (insights cycle time, decision conversion) while leadership owns the business outcome (enterprise NRR, ACV growth).
2. **Slack growth team** owns product outcomes (team activation at ~2,000 messages, channel-creation rate) while finance owns the business outcome (paid-team conversion, ARR growth).
3. **Helm Labs** — pre-launch, the product outcome was "qualified meetings booked per week"; the business outcome ($3.25M pipeline) followed from that product outcome.

## Anti-patterns
1. **Owning revenue without pricing power.** A team without pricing/packaging authority cannot own ARR. Asking it to is a setup for failure.
2. **Output as outcome.** "Ship the new onboarding" is an output. The product outcome is "increase activation from 32% to 50%" — the onboarding is one bet toward that outcome.
