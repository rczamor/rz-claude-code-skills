# Intrapreneurship Adaptation

## What it is
A reframing template for applying the Six Forcing Questions to internal company projects (intrapreneurship, new-initiative pitches, internal tools, "innovation lab" work). The forcing questions still apply — but two of them (Q4 and Q6) need different framings because the success conditions are political and structural, not market.

## Why it matters
Internal projects fail differently from startups. They don't die from no demand — they die when the VP champion leaves, when the reorg buries the team, or when the demo gets killed at the budget review. The intrapreneurship adaptation reframes the wedge and future-fit questions to match the actual failure modes.

## How to apply

**Q4 reframe — Internal Wedge:** "What's the smallest demo that gets your VP/sponsor to greenlight the project?"

The internal-project wedge isn't "smallest paid version" — it's smallest *demo-able* version that wins the next budget meeting. The deliverable is sponsor confidence, not user payment.

**Q6 reframe — Reorg Survival:** "Does this survive a reorg — or does it die when your champion leaves?"

The internal-project future-fit question isn't about market change — it's about organizational change. Projects that depend on a single sponsor are future-fragile no matter how good the user signal is.

The other four questions (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q5) apply unchanged. Demand reality, status quo, named human, and observation surprise still matter — but the user is internal, the budget is internal, and the consequences are career-shaped.

## Examples

1. **Q4 internal:** Founder pitching an internal AI-summary tool. Reframe: "What's the 5-minute demo that makes your VP say yes at next month's review? Not the full system — the version that wins the budget."
2. **Q6 internal:** Same project. Reframe: "Your sponsor is the CTO who recruited you. If she leaves in 18 months — does this project die? If yes, what makes it portable across leadership changes?"
3. **Q3 still applies:** Even internally, "marketing teams" isn't an answer. Name the actual person whose career problem this solves.

## Anti-patterns
- Skipping the diagnostic entirely for internal projects because "it's not a startup." Internal projects fail more often, not less — just differently.
- Applying the original Q4 ("smallest paid version") to an internal project. Internal users don't pay; sponsors greenlight.
- Applying the original Q6 ("market changes") to an internal project. The internal project's "market" is the org chart.
