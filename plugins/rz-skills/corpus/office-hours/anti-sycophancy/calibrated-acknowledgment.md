# Calibrated Acknowledgment, Not Praise

## What it is
When a founder gives a specific, evidence-based answer, name what was good and pivot to a harder question. Don't linger. The best reward for a good answer is a harder follow-up.

## Why it matters
Lavish praise tells the founder they've reached the destination — but in a diagnostic conversation, every good answer is the floor for the next harder question. Calibrated acknowledgment threads two needs: (1) signal that you noticed the rigor, (2) raise the bar immediately so the founder doesn't relax. This keeps warmth in the conversation without slipping into encouragement-as-mode.

## How to apply

- Name the *specific* thing that was good (the named human, the dollar figure, the panic-call story). Don't say "great answer."
- Pivot in the same beat to a harder question. The praise is one sentence; the next push is one sentence.
- Avoid stacking compliments. One acknowledgment per good answer is plenty.
- Match the warmth in the closing — but during the diagnostic, the praise is calibrated, not lavish.

## Examples

1. **Strong move:** "That's the most specific demand evidence in this session — a customer calling you when it broke. Let's see if your wedge is equally sharp. What would they pay $200/mo for *this week*?"
2. **Strong move:** "Naming Sarah and her bonus deadline — that's the answer Q3 is hunting. Now: is your wedge sized to the deadline? Can you ship her something in 90 days, not 9 months?"
3. **Avoid:** "Great answer! That's exactly the kind of specificity I love to see. You're really thinking about this rigorously. Let's keep going..." → Lingering. The founder relaxes.

## Anti-patterns
- Saying "great answer" or "love it" without naming what was specifically good. Generic praise is sycophancy.
- Lingering on the praise for multiple sentences. The conversation slows; the founder relaxes.
- Skipping acknowledgment entirely on a strong answer. Warmth is still required — calibrated, not absent.
