# The User's Words Beat the Founder's Pitch

## What it is
A non-negotiable operating principle in startup-mode office hours. There is almost always a gap between what the founder says the product does and what users say it does. The user's version is the truth. If your best customers describe your value differently than your marketing copy does, rewrite the copy.

## Why it matters
Founders spend months crafting the pitch. Users describe the product in 30 seconds, in their own vocabulary, based on what actually solved their problem. When those two descriptions diverge, the user is correct — they're the ones who paid, came back, told a friend. The founder's pitch is hypothesis; the user's words are evidence.

## How to apply

- Ask: "How do your best customers describe what you do — in their words, not yours?"
- Listen for vocabulary mismatch. If the user says "it's a glorified spreadsheet that auto-fills the boring parts" and the pitch says "AI-powered revenue intelligence platform" — the user is right.
- When the gap is large, the founder should rewrite the marketing copy to match the user's words, not push the user's words toward the marketing copy.
- Pull the user's exact phrasing into positioning and onboarding.

## Examples

1. **Pitch:** "AI-powered revenue intelligence platform." **User:** "It tells me which deals are about to die." → The user's version is the lead. Rewrite the homepage.
2. **Pitch:** "Collaborative knowledge graph for engineering teams." **User:** "It's the search that actually finds the thing." → That's the wedge. Lead with it.
3. **Pitch:** "End-to-end developer productivity suite." **User:** "It writes my standup updates so I don't have to." → Narrower, more specific, more wedge-shaped than the pitch.

## Anti-patterns
- Coaching the user toward the marketing language during interviews. Let them describe it badly — that's the data.
- Treating user vocabulary as imprecise versions of the "real" product description. The user is more accurate than the pitch.
- Keeping marketing copy that no real user would recognize. If your customer wouldn't say it, don't print it.
