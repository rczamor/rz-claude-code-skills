# Watch, Don't Demo

## What it is
A non-negotiable operating principle in startup-mode office hours. Guided walkthroughs teach you nothing about real usage. Sitting behind someone while they struggle — and biting your tongue — teaches you everything. If you haven't done this, that's assignment #1.

## Why it matters
Demo calls are theater. The founder drives, the user nods, both leave with confirmation bias intact. Real observation is the opposite: the user drives, the founder watches, and the silence reveals every place the product fails. The discipline is biting your tongue — every coaching nudge erases the data you came to collect.

## How to apply

- Sit a real user in front of the product with a real task and no instructions. Watch in silence.
- Bite your tongue. Don't help. Don't explain. Don't coach. Every word you say erases data.
- Take notes on every place they hesitate, click the wrong thing, ask "wait, where's...", or do something the product wasn't designed for.
- The wrong-place clicks and the surprising emergent uses are often the real product trying to emerge.
- If you've never done this, the assignment is to do it this week — before any further building.

## Examples

1. **Demo theater:** Founder shares screen, walks user through happy path, user says "this is great" — zero learning extracted.
2. **Real observation:** User opens the app, ignores the dashboard, hits export, downloads a CSV, opens Excel, never returns. The wedge is "fast structured export," not "analytics dashboard."
3. **Tongue-biting payoff:** User struggles for 4 minutes to find the create button. Founder learns the entire navigation IA is wrong — would have missed it by demo'ing the path.

## Anti-patterns
- Calling demo calls "user research." A demo is the founder driving; observation is the user driving.
- Helping when the user gets stuck. Stuck-ness is the data.
- Treating surprising emergent usage as misuse. It's often the real product.
