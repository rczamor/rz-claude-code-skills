# The Status Quo is Your Real Competitor

## What it is
A non-negotiable operating principle in startup-mode office hours. Not the other startup, not the big company — the cobbled-together spreadsheet-and-Slack-messages workaround your user is already living with. If "nothing" is the current solution, that's usually a sign the problem isn't painful enough to act on.

## Why it matters
Founders obsess over named competitors and miss that 80% of their addressable users have never tried any product in the space — they've just hacked something together. That hack is the real competitor: it's free, it's already learned, it's emotionally owned, and switching costs are real. Beating an Airtable-and-Zapier hack is harder than beating another startup, because the user built it themselves and is invested in defending it.

## How to apply

- When a founder says "our competitor is [other startup]," redirect: "Forget the startup. What does the user do *before they ever hear about either of you*?"
- Quantify the hack: hours per week, dollars per month, who maintains it, what breaks.
- If the answer is genuinely "nothing — there's no current solution" — that's a red flag. Truly painful problems leave a trail of workarounds.
- Your wedge has to be 10x better than the spreadsheet, not 10% better than the other startup.

## Examples

1. **Wrong competitor framing:** "We compete with Notion and Coda." **Reframe:** "Most of your future users still use Google Docs and Slack messages. That's the workflow you have to displace."
2. **The status quo, quantified:** "Three engineers at MidCo maintain a homegrown Airtable + Zapier hack. 12 hours/week, breaks twice a quarter, costs them a sprint each time." → Now you can size the wedge.
3. **No status quo, red flag:** "There's no current solution — that's why the opportunity is huge." → Actually, that usually means no one is in enough pain to act.

## Anti-patterns
- Building a competitive deck that names other startups but ignores spreadsheets, Slack, and email. Those are the actual incumbents.
- Treating "nothing" as opportunity. Truly painful problems already have ugly workarounds.
- Forgetting that users defend their hacks. Switching out of a homegrown system has emotional cost.
