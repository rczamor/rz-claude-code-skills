# Pattern 4 — Growth Stats → Vision Test

## What it is
A pushback pattern for when the founder cites a market growth rate ("market growing 20% YoY," "AI is exploding") as if it constituted a vision. The move is to name that growth rate is not a thesis — every competitor in the space cites the same stat — and demand the founder's *specific* claim about how the market changes in a way that makes *their* product more essential.

## Why it matters
Growth-rate citations are the laziest form of strategy. They're true for everyone in the category. A real vision says: "Here's how the user's job changes in 3 years, and here's why that change routes value to *us* specifically." Without that, the founder is surfing a tide every competitor surfs.

## How to apply

When you hear growth-rate framing:
1. Acknowledge the stat exists.
2. Name that every competitor cites it — it's not differentiating.
3. Demand the founder's specific thesis about how the user's world changes.
4. The good answer routes that change to *their* product specifically, not the category.

## Examples

**Founder:** "The market is growing 20% year over year"

**BAD:** "That's a strong tailwind. How do you plan to capture that growth?"

**GOOD:** "Growth rate is not a vision. Every competitor in your space can cite the same stat. What's YOUR thesis about how this market changes in a way that makes YOUR product more essential?"

The BAD response treats the tailwind as the strategy. The GOOD response names that tailwinds are common to all competitors and demands a *specific* thesis. The emphasis on "YOUR" is doing real work — every competitor has the *market's* thesis; only some have their own.

## Anti-patterns
- Asking "how do you plan to capture that growth" — the BAD response. It accepts tailwind as strategy.
- Accepting "AI keeps getting better so we keep getting better." That's true for every AI startup — useless as positioning.
- Letting the founder skip Q6 because "the market is growing." That's exactly the framing Q6 is designed to break.
