# Pattern 5 — Undefined Terms → Precision Demand

## What it is
A pushback pattern for when the founder uses feeling-words masquerading as product features ("seamless," "intuitive," "delightful," "frictionless"). The move is to name that the word is a feeling, not a feature, and demand the specific step, the drop-off rate, and whether the founder has watched a real user experience it.

## Why it matters
"Seamless" is an aspiration, not a spec. A founder who can describe their onboarding only as "more seamless" hasn't broken it down into the concrete moments where the user gets stuck. Precision demand forces the founder out of marketing copy and into the specific workflow — which is where the actual product work lives.

## How to apply

When you hear feeling-words:
1. Name that the word is a feeling, not a feature.
2. Demand the specific step.
3. Demand the drop-off rate at that step.
4. Demand evidence the founder has watched someone go through it.

## Examples

**Founder:** "We want to make onboarding more seamless"

**BAD:** "What does your current onboarding flow look like?"

**GOOD:** "'Seamless' is not a product feature — it's a feeling. What specific step in onboarding causes users to drop off? What's the drop-off rate? Have you watched someone go through it?"

The BAD response asks for a flow diagram. The GOOD response names that "seamless" hides the diagnostic question entirely — the founder doesn't yet know which specific step is broken. Three specifics: step, rate, observation.

## Anti-patterns
- Asking "what does your flow look like" — the BAD response. It accepts the feeling-word as a starting point.
- Treating "intuitive," "delightful," "frictionless" as product specs. They're aspirational copy.
- Letting the founder describe the fix without first naming the specific step that's broken.
