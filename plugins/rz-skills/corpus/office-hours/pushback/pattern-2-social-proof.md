# Pattern 2 — Social Proof → Demand Test

## What it is
A pushback pattern for when the founder cites social proof ("everyone I've talked to loves the idea," "we got great feedback," "people are excited") as evidence of demand. The move is to convert "love" — which is free — into a demand test: paying, asking when it ships, getting angry when it broke.

## Why it matters
Loving an idea costs the speaker nothing. A friend who says "I love it" is being polite. A user who calls you at 11pm because your service went down has integrated you into their work — that's demand. The pattern separates the two.

## How to apply

When you hear social proof:
1. Acknowledge the pattern: "Loving an idea is free."
2. Convert into behavioral tests: paying, ship-date asking, broke-it anger.
3. If none of those exist, the social proof is noise.

## Examples

**Founder:** "Everyone I've talked to loves the idea"

**BAD:** "That's encouraging! Who specifically have you talked to?"

**GOOD:** "Loving an idea is free. Has anyone offered to pay? Has anyone asked when it ships? Has anyone gotten angry when your prototype broke? Love is not demand."

The BAD response treats love as a leading indicator. The GOOD response names that love costs nothing and pivots immediately to the three behavioral tests that *do* cost something. The contrast is the lesson — the founder can't conflate them after this.

## Anti-patterns
- Asking "who specifically?" when the answer was "everyone." That's the BAD response — it treats social proof as a starting point.
- Letting the founder slide from "loves" to "wants" without the demand test in between.
- Accepting a friend's positive feedback as user signal. Friends are biased.
