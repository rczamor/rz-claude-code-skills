# Q3 — Desperate Specificity

## What it is
The third forcing question. It refuses category-level answers ("healthcare enterprises," "SMBs," "marketing teams") and demands a *named human* with a title and a consequence they actually face if the problem isn't solved.

**Ask:** "Name the actual human who needs this most. What's their title? What gets them promoted? What gets them fired? What keeps them up at night?"

## Why it matters
You can't email a category. Founders who can't name a real person are usually building for an imagined user assembled from market research, not a person whose Tuesday afternoon they've witnessed. The category-level answer is the polished pitch; the named human is the truth.

## How to apply

**Push until you hear:** A name. A role. A specific consequence they face if the problem isn't solved. Ideally something the founder heard directly from that person's mouth.

**Red flags:** Category-level answers. "Healthcare enterprises." "SMBs." "Marketing teams." These are filters, not people. You can't email a category.

**Forcing exemplar:**

SOFTENED (avoid): "Who's your target user, and what gets them to buy? Worth thinking about before marketing spend ramps."

FORCING (aim for): "Name the actual human. Not 'product managers at mid-market SaaS companies' — an actual name, an actual title, an actual consequence. What's the real thing they're avoiding that your product solves? If this is a career problem, whose career? If this is a daily pain, whose day? If this is a creative unlock, whose weekend project becomes possible? If you can't name them, you don't know who you're building for — and 'users' isn't an answer."

The pressure is in the stacking — don't collapse it into a single ask. The specific consequence (career / day / weekend) is **domain-dependent**: B2B tools name career impact; consumer tools name daily pain or social moment; hobby / open-source tools name the weekend project that gets unblocked. Match the consequence to the domain, but never let the founder stay at "users" or "product managers."

## Examples

1. **Category answer to push:** "Marketing teams at mid-market SaaS companies." → "Name one. One person. One company. One thing they said to you."
2. **Strong answer:** "Sarah, VP Marketing at Acme. She got the job last quarter and her board wants attribution clarity in 90 days. If she can't show pipeline-to-revenue mapping, her bonus tanks." → Specific person, specific career consequence, specific deadline.
3. **Consumer-domain answer:** "My friend Jordan rebuilds his Spotify playlists every Sunday morning because the algorithm forgets what he loved last week." → A name, a day, a small but real recurring pain.

## Anti-patterns
- Accepting "product managers at SaaS companies" as an answer. That's a filter, not a person.
- Collapsing the stack ("name them and tell me their title") into a single ask. The pressure is in the stacking.
- Demanding career consequence for a consumer or hobby product. Match the consequence to the domain.
