# Q1 — Demand Reality

## What it is
The first forcing question in the YC product diagnostic. It separates real demand from noise — interest, signups, "that's interesting" — by pressing the founder to name behavior that proves someone would be upset if the product disappeared tomorrow.

**Ask:** "What's the strongest evidence you have that someone actually wants this — not 'is interested,' not 'signed up for a waitlist,' but would be genuinely upset if it disappeared tomorrow?"

## Why it matters
Most pre-product startups die because the founder mistook interest for demand. Waitlists, VC enthusiasm, supportive friends — none of these survive the moment a user has to choose between your product and the cobbled workaround they already have. Q1 forces the founder to confront whether anyone has crossed from "thinks it's neat" into "behaves as if they need it."

## How to apply

**Push until you hear:** Specific behavior. Someone paying. Someone expanding usage. Someone building their workflow around it. Someone who would have to scramble if you vanished.

**Red flags:** "People say it's interesting." "We got 500 waitlist signups." "VCs are excited about the space." None of these are demand.

**After the founder's first answer to Q1, check their framing before continuing:**

1. **Language precision:** Are the key terms in their answer defined? If they said "AI space," "seamless experience," "better platform" — challenge: "What do you mean by [term]? Can you define it so I could measure it?"
2. **Hidden assumptions:** What does their framing take for granted? "I need to raise money" assumes capital is required. "The market needs this" assumes verified pull. Name one assumption and ask if it's verified.
3. **Real vs. hypothetical:** Is there evidence of actual pain, or is this a thought experiment? "I think developers would want..." is hypothetical. "Three developers at my last company spent 10 hours a week on this" is real.

If the framing is imprecise, **reframe constructively** — don't dissolve the question. Say: "Let me try restating what I think you're actually building: [reframe]. Does that capture it better?" Then proceed with the corrected framing. This takes 60 seconds, not 10 minutes.

## Examples

1. **Soft answer that should get pushed:** "We have 500 people on the waitlist." → "Waitlists are the cheapest commitment a person can make. Has anyone called you when something broke? Has anyone offered to pay before you've shipped? Those are demand."
2. **Strong answer:** "A customer called me at 11pm when the staging environment went down for 20 minutes." → That's the gold. Name what was good and pivot to a harder question.
3. **Hypothetical to push on:** "I think marketing teams would want this." → "Hypothetical. Have you watched a marketing team struggle with this exact problem? Whose team? What did they do instead?"

## Anti-patterns
- Accepting the polished first answer. The real answer comes after the second or third push.
- Treating waitlist size as demand. A waitlist measures curiosity, not commitment.
- Letting "VCs are excited" stand in for user pull. Investor enthusiasm is not user behavior.
