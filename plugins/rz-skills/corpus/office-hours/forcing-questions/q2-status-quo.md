# Q2 — Status Quo

## What it is
The second forcing question in the YC product diagnostic. It surfaces what users are *already doing* to solve the problem — even badly — and quantifies the cost of that workaround. The status quo is the real competitor, not another startup.

**Ask:** "What are your users doing right now to solve this problem — even badly? What does that workaround cost them?"

## Why it matters
If no one is doing anything about a problem, the problem probably isn't painful enough to act on. Real demand leaves a trail: cobbled spreadsheets, duct-taped tools, hours spent manually, people hired to do the job by hand, internal tooling maintained by engineers who'd rather be building product. That trail is what your wedge gets to displace. If you can't find it, you're inventing a problem.

## How to apply

**Push until you hear:** A specific workflow. Hours spent. Dollars wasted. Tools duct-taped together. People hired to do it manually. Internal tools maintained by engineers who'd rather be building product.

**Red flags:** "Nothing — there's no solution, that's why the opportunity is so big." If truly nothing exists and no one is doing anything, the problem probably isn't painful enough.

**Push moves:**
1. Demand a specific workflow, not a category. "How exactly does Sarah do this on Tuesdays?"
2. Convert the workaround into hours-per-week or dollars-per-month. Founders without numbers haven't actually watched.
3. If the founder names a competitor product, redirect: "Forget the other startup. What does your user do *before they ever hear about you*?"

## Examples

1. **Soft answer:** "There's no real solution out there." → Red flag. Either the problem isn't real, or the founder hasn't looked. Push: "What did the last 10 people you talked to do *before* you showed up?"
2. **Strong answer:** "Three engineers at MidCo spent 12 hours a week maintaining an internal Airtable + Zapier hack. That hack breaks twice a quarter and costs them a sprint each time." → Quantified. Specific. The wedge is now visible.
3. **Mid answer to push further:** "They use spreadsheets." → "Whose spreadsheet? How many tabs? Who updates it? What breaks?"

## Anti-patterns
- Treating "no current solution" as a green light. It's almost always a sign the pain isn't real.
- Naming a competing startup as the status quo. The status quo is what users do *without any product at all*.
- Accepting category-level workarounds ("they use spreadsheets") without forcing the specific workflow.
